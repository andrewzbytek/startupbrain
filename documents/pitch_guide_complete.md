# Part 1: Storytelling Fundamentals

## Why storytelling is important when pitching to VCs

Pitching your story to VCs can be daunting. You'll be sitting in the proverbial spotlight, palms sweaty, armed with nothing but your idea and a slide deck that you spent far too many late nights perfecting. Well, we’re here to talk about why storytelling is not merely a narrative frill, but a crucial engine in your pitch machine—an engine that purrs, captivates, and tops up your company bank account nicely. 

There's a common misconception that venture capitalists can be Patagonia-vest-wearing calculators, making decisions based on Excel formulas and caffeine highs. What many people forget is that VCs are human beings. And human beings, no matter how logical or analytical or Patagonia-clad, have this funny penchant for stories. We’re talking millennia of oral traditions, fairy tales, epic poetry—the works. To be human is to love a good yarn, spun ever so well. 

Not only do VCs enjoy stories, but they don’t invest in abstract plans and vague dreams: They invest in other humans. That’s people who’ve lived lives, enjoyed experiences, seen love and heartbreak, and who just want to live their lives as fully as they can. People like you, in fact. Investing in a startup isn’t about checking off a laundry list of requirements: VCs want to know the character arc of the protagonist (i.e. you) as well as the thrilling plot of your business and why it exists. So, embodying your venture in a story is the process of making yourself memorable, and ideally, like a gripping novel, un-put-downable.

You may be quick on your feet and able to toss out answers like you're in an improv comedy sketch, but pitching to raise money is rarely the time for theatrics. If you don’t know an answer, say so. If you make one up, VCs will sniff out the ruse like a bloodhound on a scent. 

Take the first ten minutes or so of Pixar's 'Up': a masterclass in captivating, touching, but not-schmaltzy storytelling. You find yourself quickly caring about and understanding the characters, rooting for them, and perhaps shedding a tear for them. Making your would-be VCs cry may be ambitious and/or ill-advised, but the core point stands: you want to engage them emotionally. The more they connect with your dream, the more they'll want to facilitate it. 

When it comes to your slide deck, think of it as a tapestry that weaves together images and graphs to backdrop your performance. The deck isn’t meant to be a crutch. Rather, it’s there to accentuate your moves because you're the star. If your slide deck steals the show, then you've got problems.

Much like a comedian sets up a joke to deliver the punchline later, your pitch should consist of ‘gates’ and ‘call-backs.’ Gates are those vital tidbits that need to be established early on for the story to flow. Call-backs, meanwhile, are those moments where earlier elements resurface in a revelatory halo, leading to applause if you’re on stage, and investment when you’re in an investor’s den. Think of it as Chekhov’s gun but for fundraising.

You might not have a teleprompter, but a mental checklist serves just as well. Remember the key points you need to cover and guide your narrative towards them. This is not a ramble in the park; this is a guided tour with specific landmarks to visit. If you miss one, your audience will end up lost and confused.

Storytelling isn't the icing on the cake when it comes to raising venture capital; it’s the whole darn bakery. It's your hook, your appeal, and your emotional connection to your potential investors. So, when you pitch, don't just present; perform. Spin a tale so enchanting they won't just want to invest; they'll want an encore.

## Three points to remember 

  * VCs are human, and humans love a good story

  * Use storytelling tools to bring your startup to life

  * Keep your audience in mind: and to do that, you have to understand them.

## Understanding your audience

Stories come in all sorts of forms: bedtime stories, blockbuster movies, paintings, photos, and startup pitches. They each have different characters, different story arcs, different motivations, and different audiences. When you tell a story, whatever the medium, you gotta keep your audience in mind. 

Who’s listening? What do they care about? What are their needs?

Storytelling is an art, but when it comes to pitching your startup, it’s also a business tactic. It’s not just about weaving together the narrative of your startup and making it irresistible to an investor, but recognising and responding to an investor’s interests, cues, and reactions. When it comes to pitching, you’re not just a founder and a narrator. You also need to be an entertainer, an educator, and (as far as you can pull it off) a mind-reader.

Let's consider the VC partner, our story consumer for today. This individual likely woke up to an inbox more crowded than a Black Friday sale at a gadget store. They've already sat through a board meeting, analyzed a quarterly report from a startup, and heard pitches from entrepreneurs who think they've cracked the code of teleporting pet hamsters or blockchain-enabled toothbrushes. They're flooded with information, might be distracted, and possibly dangerously caffeinated. They want to be there, listening to your pitch. It’s their job, and they wouldn’t have taken the meeting if they didn’t think you had _something_ going for you. You made it this far - well done - now you need to make yourself stand out from the masses.

You might think your startup is the next sliced bread, but the investor sees bread all day. They want to know if your bread comes with Nutella, and if that Nutella will make them a boatload of money. To them, your business is not just a vision but an investment vehicle that should speed along the highway, destination Profitville. While you may want to delve into the intricate design features of your AI-powered, cloud-based, eco-friendly bagel slicer, they're thinking: "How fast can this thing scale?" and "Is there even a big market for precisely sliced bagels?"

Investors have biases. They are humans; all humans have biases, however precise and logical we might want ourselves to be. It’s your job, as a storyteller, to be alert to these biases and tap into them. 

So, tailor your story like a Savile Row suit. Elsewhere we’ll cover [how VC works](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/119652/lessons/367313>), so we won’t dip too deeply into that here - but remember this: Your investor wants to dream with you, of an enormous financial success. That means an acquisition (i.e. another company buying yours) or a listing on the stock market (usually through an initial public offering - or an IPO). 

So think about this: How do companies get successful? It’s the right team, building the right product that solves a problem a significant number of people are willing to pay good money to solve. That means being in a market that’s big and ideally growing. That means having a great team who can do this work. It’s your job to tell the story of how you think all of this is going to pan out. 

You see, VCs are not just investing in your product; they're investing in you and your story. It's like dating, but with term sheets and due diligence. They need to know you're the one, the Neo of bagel-slicing solutions, destined to disrupt breakfast routines across the globe.

But it’s not just a case of a VC believing that you can lead your startup to success. They also have to believe that you are someone they can work with. If your pitch is successful, they will likely be taking a board seat. That means they’ll be working with you–advising you, challenging you, telling you things you don’t want to hear, and celebrating with you–for the lifetime of the investment. If you come across as a surly and arrogant know-it-all, why would they bother? Be confident, yes. But be receptive, be friendly. Show that you are able to receive advice, process it, and make choices accordingly. 

To sum it up, storytelling in the pitching world is more than mere information transfer. It's a choreographed performance of persuasion, designed to meet your audience precisely where they are—be it an emotional state, an intellectual realm, or a financial craving. Your audience isn't just listening to your story; they're deciding whether to make your story a part of their own. 

## Three points to remember 

  * A story needs to be tailored to its audience. Make sure you understand VC as an asset class. (We have [a lesson about that](<https://haje.pub/pg-venture-scale>) coming up!) 

  * Be receptive to your audience. Take your cues from them, adapting your tone and adjusting your focus in response to their questions, facial expressions and body language.

  * VCs aren’t just investing in your startup, they are investing in you. You need to show yourself to be collaborative, coachable, and someone they can work with.

# Part 2: Venture Capital and How It Works

## How venture capital works

The world of venture capital is populated by venture capitalists (VCs), limited partners (LPs), and you, startup founders. 

Understanding the dynamic of how the startup and VC ecosystem works is really important—knowing the business model of the people you are about to work with helps you understand what they need. That also means you can target your stories better. 

As a startup founder, you spend a lot of time understanding your customers. You have to, right? Think of VCs as a potential $10 million customer: You’d make damn sure that you understand what makes them tick, and what the ‘problems’ are which they are experiencing. 

* * *

We have a handy [glossary](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/glossary/the-pitch-guide-fundraising-glossary>) that defines the terms and descriptors which are used throughout the course and generally in the world of venture capital, should you need to refer to it.

* * *

### **Limited partners (LPs)**

Limited partners, or LPs, are the people or organizations with the money. They can be pension funds, university endowments, high net-worth individuals, so-called ‘family offices,’ or even government agencies. They usually have giant pots of money, and with a small slice of that money, they do high-risk investing in the hope that they can grow their overall bank balance at least at the pace of inflation, but ideally a bit more than that, too. 

To do that, they have a ton of things they could invest in. They put some money into "Oh-so-stable" assets like bonds, some into "Let's-see-what-happens" stock markets, and a pinch into the "YOLO" bucket of venture capital, crypto, hedge funds, and Pokemon cards. Okay, fine, maybe not those. 

While they might have the money, they don’t necessarily have the time or the expertise to make the decisions about which startup to back and which to steer clear of. They certainly don’t have the time to do the board work to help develop these startups as they march towards an exit. Instead, they give a chunk of their wealth to venture capital funds to invest on their behalf.

### **Venture capital funds (VCs) and the people who work there**

So what do venture capital firms do? These entities, often partnerships, serve as scouts on the financial battlefield, investing on behalf of the LPs. The partners in a VC fund are known as general partners (GPs). These folks are the bosses who have the fiduciary responsibility to their investors, and ultimately, the buck stops with them. 

While they are primarily there to dish out their LPs’ cash, they usually invest some of their own money into the fund, too. 

The GPs are the ones making the decisions, looking through the proverbial binoculars to spot the next Uber before it even knows it wants to fight taxis. But they don't just do this willy-nilly. They do this armed with a set of principles known as their investment thesis—more on that in just a moment. 

You’ll probably meet a bunch of other characters in your journey through VC land as well. This could include analysts, associates, principals, junior partners, operating partners, and a few other job titles that exist, too. They are all part of the investment team, finding startups, evaluating them, investing, and looking after them as part of the portfolio. 

Many VCs also have ‘platform teams,’ that help startups with other stuff. Some have recruiters, marketers, engineering teams, fractional finance people—people who can help startup founders build more successful companies, faster. They will be out there looking for startups and passing them off to the investment team, but they aren’t usually part of the investment process itself. 

### **Investment thesis**

An investment thesis is, essentially, the business plan for a venture capital fund. It sets out where and how it wishes to invest its money based on what is important to those involved with the fund. This ‘thesis’ is what the VCs use to raise money from their LPs. In a nutshell: “Hey, we are planning to raise $100 million to invest according to this set of ideas. We think that is a great opportunity for these reasons.”

These can be extremely broad, or very focused, and sometimes, confusingly, both at once. At the very least, an investment thesis will spell out the industry, stage, business models and geography the VC will be investing in. 

**Industry** : This could mean investing in MedTech, AdTech, IoT, AgTech, PropTech, Blockchain, AI, or any number of other Techs. Some investors focus very deeply on one or two, and others invest more broadly. 

**Stage** : They vary from "We-have-an-idea-but-need-money-for-ramen" (usually referred to as pre-seed investing) to "We-are-so-big-now-we-need-to-build-a-spaceship." (Growth stage, or series B and beyond). Earlier investments tend to be smaller sums of money into companies with higher risk of failure, but with the potential of a higher reward. Later stage investments tend to be larger, as they are funding growth, but less risky.

**Business models:** Different product types have different business models and different risks associated with them. Some investors will only invest in software-as-a-service (SaaS) products such as Slack. Others focus on consumer packaged goods (CPG) such as Dollar Shave Club. Others may only invest in Internet-of-Things models with a ‘you buy the hardware then subscribe to the software’ model. If your investor only invests in one, don’t bother trying to pitch them something else. 

**Geography** : Investors may choose different approaches to geography. Many investors will only invest in companies in their own country, on the same continent, or in the same time zone or city. 

**Special interests** : Finally, some investors may choose to invest in founders based on criteria that don’t fit any of the above. Perhaps they focus on alumni from a particular university, or investing in underrepresented groups, or with a goal of having a particular impact (companies that solve aspects of homelessness or climate, for example). 

### **Picky, picky, picky**

VC firms can—and, frankly, should—be very particular, with criteria ranging from the very broad to the microscopically specific. Think of it like dating apps but for money. Just as you wouldn't date someone who doesn't meet your criteria (hopefully), VC firms aren't going to swipe right on a startup that doesn't align with their thesis. 

An important piece to remember here is that the VCs have a responsibility to their investors, and depending on how the company does its thing, investing off-thesis could be fantastically frowned upon. In other words: If the LPs signed up for developing cancer therapies, they won't be thrilled if their money ends up funding a revolutionary new dog-walking app.

So, it behooves you to know your potential investor's investment thesis, so you know whether you’re a good fit. 

Personally, I would always ask who the LPs in a fund are, too. They may not want to tell you exactly (there’s politics involved, sometimes), but it can’t harm to ask if you’re curious. 

### **The end of the road**

Ultimately, the way the VCs see a return on their investments, is when a startup has an ‘exit,’ or basically, when it becomes possible to turn the shares of the company into cold hard cash again. 

To understand an exit is to glimpse the pearly gates of venture capitalist heaven. An exit is the culmination of years of dedication, hard work, and taking chances, albeit informed ones. A startup, once a fledgling idea, matures into something wonderful and is either bought out (acquisition) or goes public (IPO). 

### **How they keep the lights on**

Now, managing a venture firm isn't all champagne and playa dust. The firm has bills. Staff salaries, legal fees, office space, and mundane but essential things like internet connections and a supply of Lara bars. So how does a venture firm afford it all? 

Enter the "Management Fee." Typically, this is 2% of the total assets under management (AUM), assessed annually. If you've got a $100 million fund, you're looking at a cozy $2 million a year just to keep the wheels greased. But this isn't the real moneymaker. That comes next.

### **Carried interest: the golden goose**

The pièce de résistance of venture capital remuneration is the "Carried Interest," or "carry" if you're trying to sound hip at Silicon Valley cocktail parties. The carry is essentially a performance fee, generally about 20% of the returns a VC firm gets from its investments. Think of it as a tip for a job well done, only it's more like you're tipping the waiter an entire vineyard for a bottle of wine well-served.

Picture this: A VC firm invests $100 million across a smorgasbord of 50 startups. Some crash and burn faster than a flaming asteroid, while others chug along like a punctual but quotidian commuter train, offering modest returns. Then, EUREKA! One of them explodes into the next tech unicorn, the next digital messiah, the next... you get it. That initial $2 million investment suddenly metamorphoses into $300 million. 

At the end of this hypothetical roller coaster, the $100 million fund has delivered $400 million worth of profit. Here's where the carry comes in: the VC firm takes a 20% slice, which amounts to a jaw-dropping $80 million bonus that can be distributed among the VC fund’s partners. 

### **A tricky balancing act**

Here's the kicker: Despite this potential for astronomical gains, venture capital is no get-rich-quick scheme. VCs are high-stakes gamblers playing a game of gut-churning risk. One false move, one ill-fated bet, and poof! Millions vanish into the entrepreneurial ether. It's like poker but with way more zeros and fewer smoky basements. The job of a venture capitalist is to use an almost clairvoyant blend of insight, experience, and (let’s be honest) a bit of dumb luck to pick the winners that will make up for the losers and then some.

In short, venture capital firms make their real money from the carry, the share of profits they get from each successful investment. Unless you’re a 6-year-old, unicorns are rarer than you might think (and if you are a six-year-old, I’m worried about your choice of reading materials). The vast majority of venture investments will not pan out. Thus, VCs make just enough from management fees to keep their offices flush with coffee and ideas but bet on the carry for the real payday. 

And there you have it: the somewhat convoluted, always exhilarating, and frequently absurd mechanisms through which venture capital firms turn money into, well, a lot more money. Welcome!

### **Summary: It's like a complex dance**

Venture capital is not just about throwing money at any young whippersnapper with a dream and a PowerPoint. It's a meticulously choreographed dance where both LPs and VC firms want the same thing: a massive, glorious, wallet-stuffing return on investment. They're looking for startups that fit into their well-defined theses, aiming to woo both founders and LPs.

So before you stride into that pitch meeting, PowerPoint deck in hand and dreams in your eyes, remember to study your audience. Know their likes, dislikes, and what kind of returns they're waltzing towards. Because, as in any good dance, it's not just about your moves; it's about moving in harmony with your partner.

There, you now have a front-row seat to the enthralling drama that is venture capital. As a founder or curious bystander, understanding the art of this high-risk, high-reward investment game gives you the context you need to appreciate why those investment decisions are made the way they are. It's no Jane Austen novel, but the plot twists can be just as intriguing.

## Three points to remember 

  * Limited partners (LPs) invest their money into the VC funds. 

  * All VCs have an investment thesis which outlines how much they invest in which types of startup and at what stage. 

  * Earlier investments, for example pre-seed and seed, tend to be smaller but come with more risk and therefore a higher potential return. Later stage investments are a little bit safer, and usually larger to help grow a startup.

## Who should–and who should not–be raising from VCs

Alright, so you've got a startup and you're wondering if you should court some VCs to sprinkle some of their magic money dust on your company. Great! Let's talk about when you should be dialing up Sand Hill Road and when, well, maybe you shouldn't.

### **It’s a commitment**

When you jump on the VC train, it’s a long haul, not a short hop, and it happens at breakneck speed. It’s not just one round of funding that takes you to the next stop and then you can alight. 

In the early stages of a company, that often means raising a round of funding roughly every 18 months or so until your train pulls into Profitville station where either your company becomes self-sustaining or you achieve an exit and you stroll out of the doors into the sunlit uplands (or perhaps board a new startup train). 

Your investors will be expecting you to reach that destination in a seven-to-10 year horizon. Are you prepared to embark on this journey, to keep that engine stoked and those wheels greased for all that time, and will it actually get you where you need to go?

The thing about a VC-backed startup is that it doesn't get to slow down. Once you raise, you’ve got to keep going. 

### **Can you grow?**

The key thing is to be VC fundable, you have to have a company that can grow very quickly indeed. Startup accelerator Y Combinator says it looks for 15% growth per week at the early stages. Compounding growth at 15% is fiercely difficult: Is there a world where your company could potentially be doing that? What does that look like? 

If you couldn’t be a hyper-growth company, you’re in the wrong business. By all means, start your company, work your arse off, and make a good living. But if you can’t model out what hyper growth looks like for your team, in your industry, with your product? VC’s not for you. 

### **A word on lifestyle businesses**

Companies that can’t grow at the rate that VCs need, are often called ‘lifestyle businesses.’ As much as I hate how flippant that sounds, there’s something to the expression. 

If your business is chill and generating a steady cash flow but not necessarily exponential growth, VC funding might not be your jam. For instance, say you own a chain of fitness studios. You might grow—you may even grow quite fast—but it’s rare that those types of extremely op/ex heavy businesses will grow at a rate that'll satisfy a VC's appetite for massive, relatively quick returns.

### **Can you bootstrap?**

Some businesses just don't need a ton of cash to get going. We're talking small-scale software services, consulting, maybe even some types of content creation. In these cases, giving away equity for VC funding is like using a chainsaw to cut a slice of bread. Overkill, man—if you can bootstrap (i.e. grow with your own funding), that’s often a good call: You keep control of the business, and you can always choose to raise investment later. 

### **Summary**

To sum it up, if you're set up for extreme growth, need networks, and could use some sage advice, go ring some VC doorbells. If not, don’t. 

## Three points to remember 

  * For a startup to be VC-fundable, it needs to be able to grow extraordinarily quickly. 

  * With their money comes a VC’s advice and guidance. If, for whatever reason, you aren’t ready to share control or are in a space where that’s not viable, then VC isn’t for you.

  * How much do you actually need? VC funding is big outlays for even bigger returns. If you don’t need to, jumping on the VC treadmill might not be worth it—yet.

# Part 3: Introduction to the Pitch Deck

## Why you need a pitch deck

It might feel as if VC money sprouts as abundantly as fungi in a damp forest and all you need to do is turn up and pick them. But the truth is, you need to know where you’re going in the forest to find the mushrooms you can eat and you need to be fully equipped for the expedition. For that, you need a pitch deck. If you’ve read _Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy_ , it’s the equivalent of a towel: don’t go anywhere near VC without one. 

We’ve already talked about [knowing your audience](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/119939/lessons/403888>), and how you need to adapt your story and your storytelling technique depending on who’s listening. Think about it: the Alicia Silverstone film ‘Clueless’ is Jane Austen’s _Emma_ , reworked for a late 20th century audience. A big part of making your story work for different listeners is adapting your language. When it comes to VCs, they are skilled and experienced, but not necessarily in the intricate details of your area. Be straightforward. 

The VC pitch deck is a surgical instrument designed for precision. It allows you to make your point without going around in circles like a dog chasing its tail. 

Not all pitches will actually involve a pitch deck—in fact, many do not—but almost all investors will want to see a deck anyway, because that’s how they can ensure that you fit their investment thesis, and decide whether or not to take a meeting. 

During your pitch, you may want to have the deck on standby. Most of your story, you’ll be able to tell just with words, but if you have graphs, charts, or images of what you’re building, photos or renders of prototypes and the like, it’s helpful to be able to convey that visually. Finally, financials are hard to share in words—you probably won’t spend a lot of time on the full financial spreadsheet in an initial pitch meeting, but being able to share at least the top-level numbers (in the form of an operating plan) is helpful. 

We all know that numbers are the foundation of the business world. Your pitch deck should be liberally sprinkled with them. A chart going up and to the right is like a laser pointer to a cat. They have to know that people don’t just want this product, but need it, and that you can bring it to them at scale and with a profit. Don’t just use your words; use your numbers.

### **You may need more than one deck**

Ideally, you can use the same deck to send ahead of a meeting, during, and after the meeting. But sometimes, that’s not how things shake out: If you need to tease a bit, and hold back some proprietary information ahead of time, that’s okay. 

If you want a presentation deck that has fewer words (you’ll be talking, after all) and perhaps some additional photos, graphs, and charts? That is okay. 

And perhaps your leave-behind deck needs additional depth, and an appendix—remember what questions you were asked, and drop those into the appendix so your would-be investors have everything they need. 

The send-ahead and leave-behind decks need to make sense without the benefit of your voice-over. The deck you use for pitching; well—you’re using it for pitching, so it’s there as a backdrop and to add additional context. You don’t want to just be reading out what’s on the slides, so figure out how you can augment and enhance your narrative: That’s what goes on the slides. 

### **Tl;dr - The executive summary**

You could argue that the whole deck is a summary, but you want something that will stick in the minds of your potential investors long after you’ve left the building. Your executive summary is the tl;dr (too long; didn't read) of your pitch deck. 

Make it snappy; something that grabs their attention and doesn’t let go. It should encapsulate your vision, your plan, and why they should invest, all while being shorter than this paragraph. It’s usually slide 2 or the last-but-one slide of your deck—or sometimes both. 

## Three points to remember 

  * You won’t always need a deck when you are pitching, but you’ll need one before and after you get a meeting. 

  * Decks are great for images and visuals—anything that isn’t easily conveyed in words—during the pitch. Have it ready, just in case. 

  * There’s nothing wrong with having more than one deck: You may need one to send ahead, one to pitch from, and one to leave behind.

## How do you decide how much money you should raise?

"How much money should I raise for my startup?" It's a bit like asking how much fuel you need for a road trip when you only have a vague idea of the destination and the efficiency of your vehicle is still a subject of passionate debate. Welcome to Startup Land!

## The Goldilocks Principle

The first rule in deciding how much to raise is that you're almost certainly going to get it wrong. Ask for too little, and you'll run out of cash faster than a teenager burns through mobile data. Raise too much, and you might give away so much equity that you're a CEO in name only. What you want is a "Goldilocks" amount: not too little, not too much, but just right.

Easy to say, I know. Let’s dive in. 

## The art of the runway

In startup jargon, the "runway" is the amount of time your business can survive on its current capital. Your immediate goal is to extend that runway until you reach a point where the business is either self-sustaining or attractive enough for another round of investment. A typical runway is 18-24 months, mostly because, for the majority of startups, that's long enough to accomplish something significant but short enough to keep you from getting too comfortable.

Now, your runway is something you track internally, but it’s not a great idea to raise for runway specifically: Your investors don’t give a damn if it takes you 12 or 24 months to hit your goal. They care that you do, ideally sooner rather than later, and that you don’t run out of cash in the process. To them, they are investing in your future milestones. See the ‘[use of funds](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/109877/lessons/367515>)’ section for a lot more about how to think about milestones in the context of a fundraise. 

## Bake in a “something will go wrong” factor

It's not a matter of "if" but "when." Something will go wrong, and you'll need a buffer for that. A key hire will quit, a crucial deal will fall through, or a pandemic will shut down the economy (as unlikely as that might seem). A common recommendation is to add a 20-50% buffer to your initial calculation.

## The power dynamic

The amount you raise also influences the balance of power between you and your investors. Each funding round eats into your equity and often comes with strings attached; you don’t give away a slice of the value of the company alone, but also some of your control of and decision-making within the business. 

Of course, you want to reduce how much equity and control you give away, but at the same time, if you raise too little, it comes with other problems. For a start, it’s stressful, and so is having to raise again so quickly after the previous round.

## What’s your Plan B?

Always, always, have a contingency plan. If you can't raise the full amount in this round, know beforehand what you'll cut or where you'll find alternative sources of funding. Perhaps there’s a logical intermediary step you can aim for? Is there a smaller set of milestones you can hit that doesn’t require the full amount?

Thinking through these scenarios beforehand prepares you for any funding curveballs that might come your way.

## The consultation loop

Don't just rely on your own estimations. Consult with mentors, advisors, and even other entrepreneurs who have gone down this path before. They can provide invaluable perspective, like telling you if your funding attire is the startup equivalent of leaving the house with your shirt inside out.

So there you have it, the labyrinthine quest to determine how much money to raise for your startup. It's part science, part art, and part wild, unadulterated guesswork, much like interpreting the assembly instructions for flat-pack furniture. But with careful planning, introspection, and a dash of expert advice, you might just arrive at a figure that’s more masterpiece than mess. 

Remember, the journey to building a successful startup is a marathon, not a sprint, so make sure you're adequately hydrated—financially speaking.

## Three points to remember 

  * When you’re working out how much you need to raise, remember to add in some contingency and also identify a Plan B in case you cannot raise as much as you would like.

  * Rather than think about how much money you need for a given period of time, think about how much you need to raise in order to achieve specific goals.

  * Remember that each time you raise money, you give away equity and control. It’s a balancing act to raise enough but maintain your own interest in the company.

# Part 4: The Core Slides

## The cover slide

The cover slide of a pitch deck is a bit like the opening credits of a movie or the first paragraph of a novel. Some people might skip it and jump straight to the action, but that’s on them. For you, this is the chance to set the stage and give the initial context for your pitch. 

The cover slide is the proverbial handshake of your pitch deck. It's the first slide an investor sees and usually includes your company name, your logo, tagline if you have one, the stage you’re at and how much you’re looking to raise, and probably your name, too. It might also feature a relevant, compelling image that gives a hint of your business's core concept. It's simple, but it's your first chance to make an impression.

You might think, "Why bother? Can't I dive right into explaining how I'm revolutionizing the cloud-based, AI-driven, gluten-free sandwich market?" Tempting, but no. The cover slide establishes the context and gives the investors a moment to shift gears from their previous meeting about blockchain-powered vegan toothbrushes. Think of it as a visual sorbet, cleansing the investor's palate before your delicious pitch course.

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are swamped with pitches, emails, and indigestible market reports day in and day out. When they see your cover slide, they're looking for a snapshot that screams "Professional!" and whispers "Intriguing." They want to know they're about to spend the next 10 to 20 minutes on something worth their time. No pressure, right?

### **What do you need to remember?**

Less is more. This isn't the place for clutter or that paragraph-long mission statement you spent a week crafting. Stick to the basics: 

### **Common pitfalls**

  1. **Overcomplicating things** : This isn't modern art; you're not trying to challenge the viewer's understanding of the universe. Keep it simple.

  2. **Irrelevant imagery** : A generic stock photo of smiling professionals won't tell the investors anything about your startup. Choose an image that's relevant to your business.

  3. **Inconsistency** : The design of the cover slide should be in harmony with the rest of the deck. It's the same story, after all.

  4. **Typos and errors** : It may seem trivial, but… Save your typos for later in the deck. First impressions matter! 

  5. **Forgetting the details** : Suggesting how much you are raising and what industry you are in on the first slide is super helpful. “Raising $5m to grow from 20k to 200k customers and from $200k ARR to $2.5m ARR” and “B2B SaaS / AI powered dev tools” isn’t a lot of info—but it helps hugely. 


In conclusion, the cover slide is not just a formality; it's your opening act. Much like an overture in a musical, it sets the tone for what's to come. Done right, it preps your audience emotionally and intellectually for the saga you're about to unfurl. Underestimate it at your peril!

## The summary slide

Far too often, people overlook their summary slide, much like not reading the instructions before assembling a piece of IKEA furniture. It looks easy and straightforward enough to jump right in, but without it, the whole thing might just come crashing down halfway through. 

If the [cover slide](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/109877/lessons/367328>) is the appetizer that whets the appetite, the summary slide is the dish that decides whether the meal will be memorable or just another to chalk up to experience.

Sometimes called an "executive summary" or "overview slide," this little gem works hand-in-glove with your cover slide to outline the mission and vision of your startup. It's not just a slide; it's a compact universe of your entire business. If someone read just this slide and understood it, they'd have a working idea of what you’re about—sort of like reading the blurb on a book cover and deducing the plot, main characters, and thematic motifs.

You might be asking, "I have a whole deck to explain things. Why cram it into one slide?" Well, investors are busy people, often drowning in a sea of pitches. The summary slide serves as a lifeboat, rescuing them with crucial information that lets them quickly decide if your business aligns with their investment thesis. This is efficient matchmaking, saving both parties time, money, and the awkwardness of a drawn-out, incompatible relationship.

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors have a laundry list, albeit a succinct one:

  1. **Name and logo** : Like the nametag on a conference attendee, it helps to quickly identify you.

  2. **Mission/vision** : The 'why' behind your startup, condensed into a sentence or two.

  3. **High-level business model** : Are you B2B, B2C, or perhaps a B2B2C trying to have it all?

  4. **One-line summary** : Your business, condensed to the length of a tweet. Or an X—whatever those things are called these days. 

  5. **Company stage** : Seed? Series A? Pre-revenue? Pre-product? Pre-anything?

  6. **Funding stage** : How many rounds have you survived—er, successfully closed?

  7. **How much do you want** : Put a number on your dreams, but keep it realistic.

  8. **Your industry** : Because ‘tech’ is too vague.

  9. **Anything else relevant** : Milestones, founding date, patents, or the blood type of your first-born (if relevant. Also, if that _is_ relevant, this is a pitch I’m going to want to see).


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

Like any good narrator, you guide your audience through the narrative. Structure it logically, perhaps starting with the name and logo, then transitioning into your mission and vision, followed by your business model. Lead the investor through the slide like a well-structured sentence, each point naturally flowing into the next.

### **Common Pitfalls**

  1. **Too much information** : This isn't the place to delve into the technicalities of your cloud-based algorithm or your sustainability initiatives. Stick to high-level details.

  2. **Not enough information** : Too vague, and you risk sounding like a scammy late-night infomercial. “It slices, it dices, it does... something!"

  3. **Overuse of buzzwords** : Saying you're a "disruptive, scalable, AI-driven solution" without context makes you sound like you threw darts at a tech jargon board.

  4. **Unclear language** : If your grandmother can't understand your one-line business summary, rewrite it.

  5. **Disorganization** : The slide should flow, almost like a short story, each point leading naturally to the next.


In the grand tale of your startup journey, the summary slide is the early chapter that hooks your readers—or investors, in this case. Get it right, and they'll stay with you till the very end, eagerly awaiting the sequel. 

Mess it up, and your story might just join the pile of other forgotten tales in the great library of startup history. It's not just a slide; it's your first step in turning your vision into a classic. So give it the gravitas it deserves, but keep it crisp and memorable.

## The problem slide

“We have a problem!” Your company’s entire reason for being is that it’s solving a problem. If you can’t identify one and describe it to your investors, then you have a much bigger problem. 

This slide is your opportunity to talk about the proverbial dragon plaguing the kingdom, the black hole threatening to consume the galaxy, or the more earthly issue that your startup aims to resolve.

### **What is the problem slide?**

You should use your problem slide to outline the significant issue or unmet need in the market that your startup aims to address. Think of it as the thunderbolt that sets the mood for the heroic quest you're about to undertake. If done correctly, it's the slide that makes everyone sit up a little straighter and think, "Tell me more, O wise one."

### **Why do you need this slide?**

You might say, "Isn't it obvious? We're solving a huge problem here!" Well, not so fast. Investors are not mind-readers nor seers of unseen troubles. They need you to articulate the problem clearly and compellingly to understand why your solution is not just another quixotic endeavor. 

Plus, defining the problem precisely can save you and the investors a lot of time. If the problem doesn't resonate with them, they'll know your startup isn't the droid they're looking for.

### **What are investors looking for?**

  1. **Scale** : If you're solving a problem only faced by left-handed, diabetic alchemists in Antarctica, that's lovely but hardly compelling for venture capital. The issue should be big, growing fast, and ideally both. 

  2. **Severity** : Some problems are nuisances; others are catastrophic. Make sure the investors feel the weight of the problem you're tackling. 

  3. **Market validation** : Data is your friend here. Numbers that indicate how many people are affected or the financial losses incurred due to this problem can make your case strong.

  4. **Unsolved or inadequately addressed issues** : Investors want to know why current solutions (if any) are not up to snuff.


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

The problem slide needs to make an impact. It should be clear and it should pique your investors’ interest that it’s both possible to solve and worth solving. 

Start by defining the problem in a sentence or two. Elaborate using data or real-world examples. Use storytelling techniques, perhaps a brief anecdote or a case study, to make it relatable and memorable. If possible, use visuals like graphs or images to underscore the scale or urgency of the issue.

### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Clarity** : Your audience shouldn't be puzzled about what the problem is after this slide. It should be as clear as a bell on a silent night.

  2. **Context** : Explain why this problem is indeed a problem. Sometimes what's obvious to you may not be to others.

  3. **Conciseness** : This isn't the place for a doctoral dissertation on the problem. Get to the point.


### **Common Pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: The "everybody's problem" problem 

Imagine declaring, "People are unhappy!" That's the startup equivalent of saying, "Water is wet!" The issue with presenting a problem that's too broad is that it dilutes its urgency. 

Saying that your product addresses a problem for "everybody" doesn't make you a hero; it makes you a bard singing a tune nobody wants to dance to. You need to be specific when you’re defining your problem. The more specific the better.

Pitfall 2: The sob story gone awry 

Now, a dash of emotional appeal is good—great, even. But lay it on too thick, and you'll turn your VC pitch into a soap opera. "Imagine missing a single episode of your favorite TV show; it's like a family member has died!" No, Roger, it's not. 

Emotional appeal should be a dash of salt on your gourmet problem description, not an entire saltshaker. Keep it genuine, and keep the theatrics for your acceptance speech at the annual 'Startup Oscars' (if such a thing exists).

Pitfall 3: "Trust me, it's a problem" 

Citing vague or nonexistent data is a glaring issue. Telling your potential investors that "everyone hates the current options" without providing any metrics is like telling someone to invest in your lemonade stand because your mom thinks it's good. 

Where are the surveys? Where's the evidence? You might as well bring in a note from your kindergarten teacher vouching for your reliability. Data: bring it.

Pitfall 4: The solution in sheep's clothing 

There are some eager beavers who can't wait until the next slide and they jump the gun. This entrepreneur introduces the solution while still on the problem slide. "People find it hard to get cabs—so we've built an app!" No, no, NO! Hold your horses. The problem slide is for the problem. The solution gets its own drumroll and fireworks on the next slide. Mixing the two is like mixing polka dots and stripes; it's just not done.

Pitfall 5: Assuming the problem needs solving 

Just because you can spot a problem doesn't mean the world is lying awake at night, pondering it with existential dread. In other words, not every problem needs solving in the eyes of your customer or investor. 

People will go out of their way to make sure they buy painkillers from the pharmacy. Vitamins? They rarely change their course or leave the house to buy some when they run out. As a startup, be more like a painkiller than a vitamin. 

You absolutely need to validate that this is a problem that needs solving.

Pitfall 6: People aren’t solving the problem now

Don’t confuse mild annoyances with actual problems. If, in a customer interview, you ask ‘how are you currently solving this problem,’ and the answer is a shrug and a ‘the office manager takes care of it,’ that means you need to talk to the office manager. If they say ‘eh, it’s a part of my job, no big deal,’ you have a problem: You’ve found a problem that exists, but that people aren’t really willing to pay to get a solution to. 

### **Summary**

In summary, the problem slide is where you set the stakes of your epic saga. It's where you show that there's a menacing dragon to slay, a kingdom to save, and why you—and only you—are the knight in shining armor poised to do it. 

Get this slide right, and you've laid the groundwork for the rest of your pitch. Get it wrong, and you might as well be tilting at windmills. So wield your proverbial sword of data and storytelling wisely, brave entrepreneur, and may the odds be ever in your favor.

## The solution slide

You've set the stage with the problem slide, and now, dear visionary, it's time to reveal your masterstroke with your solution slide. It’s the equivalent of a magician pulling a rabbit out of a hat, but with less magic and less animal cruelty. Here's what you need to know.

### **Why do you need this slide?**

Imagine you've made it clear to everyone in the room that the kitchen is on fire. The "solution slide" is where you state the obvious: The solution is to extinguish the fire. Note that you wouldn't, at this point, explain exactly _how_ you are going to do that. That’s what the product slide is for. 

As Steve Blank (he’s the brains behind the theory of the Lean Startup) would define it, a startup is a temporary organization in search of a repeatable business model. The key part of that definition is the “searching” aspect — which means that you are essentially building a company that is nimble and flexible enough to be able to experiment way faster than any of its competitors can. As an early-stage startup, you’ve assembled an incredible team that is uniquely qualified and deeply knowledgeable about a particular market. From its market knowledge, the team has formulated a problem it has observed in the market and wants to solve. The ‘solution’ is the search space that you’re exploring. 

The reason why you separate your product from the ‘solution’ is subtle but important: As a company, you exist to find a solution to a problem. The exact implementation of that solution might change over time. Many a startup has scrapped its product and attacked a problem from a different angle. The idea is that if you need to pivot further down the road, you can pivot on the product layer of your company, but you typically wouldn’t pivot on the solution layer. 

The difference is important because, especially at the earliest stages, if you are able to convince your investors that the market size is huge, the problem is real, and the solution makes sense, you leave the door ajar for your company to try to solve this in a few different ways.

In a nutshell - the solution part of the story outlines the strategy for solving the [problem](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/109877/lessons/367332>). Your product slide is the tactical implementation of the strategy to solve the problem. 

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are not unlike Goldilocks; they're looking for a solution that's "just right." Not too simple that it's trivial, and not too complicated that it would require a decade and a team of rocket scientists to deploy. Here are some aspects they pay attention to:

  1. **Good solution** : Is this solution a reasonable resolution to the problem described? 

  2. **Innovativeness** : What is the insight you / your team had that makes this a solution that’s novel and innovative? 

  3. **Feasibility** : Ideas are a dime a dozen, but can this actually work? Don’t go into too much detail, but paint an outline here. 


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

The solution slide works in tandem with your problem slide. One of them builds up the tension and the other releases it. Whether that’s in an explosion or a gentle exhalation is up to you, but however you do it, it should be dramatic yet believable. Here's how you can do it:

  1. **Simplicity** : Present your solution as simply as possible. If it takes a flowchart with 50 boxes to explain, it’s likely that you don’t fully understand the problem space as well as you need to. 

  2. **Keep it strategic** : It’s a fine line to walk, but keep this part of the story at a high level. 

  3. **Visual aids** : If you can outline how you fit into the market graphically, this might be a good idea here, too. Be careful to stay clear of the competitive landscape or market size - you’ll have separate slides for that. Make clear what aspects of the solution you’re going after. 


### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Differentiation** : Make sure you articulate how your solution is different from existing ones.

  2. **Keep it focused** : As a founder, you’ll often know a thousand different ways to tackle a given problem. This isn’t a brainstorm: It’s a focused strategy. Keep it focused. 

  3. **Real-world application** : Always tie it back to how it solves the problem you've highlighted.


### **Common pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: Getting too specific

The ‘solution’ slide defines the search space for your company. Where are you going to start looking for a solution to the problem? Later on, in the product slide, you’ll get to go into much more detail - but for this slide, you’ll want to lay out the 30,000-ft view of the solution space. 

The best way to think about a solution is the strategic-level response to a particular problem. It’s the thesis of the company: It found a problem it wants to solve, and it believes it has an overall solution. Now, there may be a number of ways of implementing that solution, and the company is still trying to figure out the best product-market fit for its specific implementation, but the idea, in broad strokes, is there.

Pitfall 2: "It's just better, okay?" vagueness 

Your solution is better, faster, stronger, but can you articulate why? If your differentiators are as hazy as a San Francisco morning, then you've got work to do. "Trust us, it's revolutionary," is about as convincing as a palm reader forecasting your startup's success. Quantify the advantages of your approach. 

Pitfall 3: Your solution is for a problem that doesn’t really exist

Now, you might think that this would have come up in the problem section. But so often, people start with a solution that it’s worth reminding you here that your solution can be all-singing and all-dancing, but it if it isn’t actually solving a problem that is causing some kind of stress for people that needs to be alleviated, it’s not really a solution at all.

### **Summary**

The solution slide is where you change gears from "Why you should listen to me" to "Why you should believe in me." Remember, what you're selling here isn't just a product or a service, but a vision—a tantalizing glimpse into a future where the problem you've described no longer exists. Do it right, and you won't just get applause; you'll get investors eagerly queuing up to join you on this adventure.

## The product slide

The product slide is the point in your deck where you transition from talking about problems and solutions to finally showcasing the star of the show—your product. 

Think of your product as the tactical side of the more strategic ‘solution’ slide. This is where you get to share the specifics of what you’re building and how it makes your customers’ lives better. 

### Why do you need this slide?

This is the slide where you demonstrate you're not just a starry-eyed dreamer with a lofty vision; you've got something tangible (or at least conceptual) to offer. It confirms that you're aware of your customers' wants and needs, and you're in sync with your investors' thesis. Think of it as your movie trailer. Too cryptic? The audience may lose interest. Too detailed? You’re wasting your time. Just right? You've got them lining up for the premiere.

### What are investors looking for?

As a startup founder, there’s often a temptation to get way too detailed about the product. Your investors usually care how you think about your [solution](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/109877/lessons/367353>) more than the specifics of your product. Yes, it has to be good and solve your customers’ problems, but you’re (probably) not selling your product to your investors. 

The investors care about what your product does only to the extent that it helps your customers resolve their problems. Focus on the technical innovations and the selling points to your customers. 

### How do you tell this part of the story?

  1. **Show, don’t tell** : This is your chance to show off what you’ve built to date. If you’re building hardware, show photos of the current state, and renders of what you’re working on. If it’s software, show screenshots and/or wireframes. 

  2. **Customer alignment** : Briefly describe how your product meets customer needs. Maybe showcase some testimonials or usage stats if you have them.

  3. **Uniqueness** : What sets your product apart from the thousand other apps, gadgets, or services out there?

  4. **Scalability** : Give them a glimpse of how this product can grow or adapt over time.


### What do you need to remember?

  1. **Sell the company, not just the product** : You're not a salesperson in a mall kiosk; you're a visionary selling an entire journey. The product is merely a significant mile marker along the way. Your fundraising is about the future as much as it is about the present, so consider telling the investors what you’re about to build as much as what you’ve done so far. 

  2. **Good enough is enough** : You might think that your product has to be as polished as a royal crown. But remember, venture capitalists are not shopping for crowns. They're looking for diamonds in the rough that can be chiseled into something spectacular.

  3. **Be ready for change** : Your product is not set in stone, nor should it be. Whether it's a slight pivot or a complete overhaul, change is not only possible but often likely. Investors understand that, so don’t worry too much about showing rough prototypes or drafts. 


### Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: The "it's like Uber, but for x" trope 

Analogies are like siren song: we’re overwhelmingly drawn to them. While they are tempting and feel compelling, comparatives like "It's like Uber, but for pet grooming," can often minimize the originality of your idea. Your product is its own celestial body, not an asteroid in the orbit of another. While it's understandable you'd want to hitch your wagon to a shooting star, make sure your product shines on its own merits.

Pitfall 2: Suffocated in a feature avalanche 

This happens when your slide is so jam-packed with bullet points it looks as if it's spent the afternoon at a typographical firing range. "Our product can do this, and this, oh, and this too, did we mention this?" 

While you might be tempted to cram in every nifty feature, the end result is a cluttered mess that leaves your audience with the impression of chaos, not capability. Choose three to five key features that illustrate your product's core functions and differentiators. Your audience will thank you, your slide designer might just send you a bouquet, and chances are your investor doesn't care about the depths of details to begin with. 

Pitfall 3: Stuck in a techno-babble quagmire 

As you hover over your keyboard, you may be consumed by the desire to showcase your intellectual prowess by employing words like "synergy," "blockchain," or "quantum." Resist. 

The purpose of the product slide is to explain, not to befuddle.

Pitfall 4: The undercooked demo 

Live demos are the high-risk, high-reward darling of many a pitch deck. When it works, it's like fireworks on the Fourth of July. When it doesn't, it's more like a soggy firecracker that just sort of fizzles and pops sadly. 

Unless you're 100% certain your demo will operate smoother than a velvet-lined elevator at the time of your pitch, consider using screenshots or a brief pre-recorded video. It’s an easy means to keep your cortisol levels at a safe reading - and you’ll get a chance to demo later anyway. 

Remember that the investors are investing in the future of your company. A demo is, per definition, only of what you’ve built so far. Spend a bit of time here, but keep the focus on what’s next. 

Pitfall 5: The ignored user experience 

User experience is to a product what a plot is to a movie. Sure, you could have stunning visuals and A-list actors, but if the storyline is as engaging as a legislative filibuster, you've lost your audience. 

If your product slide doesn't touch on user experience—how easy it is to engage with your product, what problem it solves in an efficient manner, etc.—then you're missing a critical piece of the puzzle.

### **Conclusion**

In conclusion, the product slide is a balancing act between the exhilarating and the pragmatic, a chance to showcase your offering while subtly hinting at the brilliant minds and market savvy that have brought it to this stage. 

It's your audition for the big leagues and—hopefully—your ticket to a future filled with opportunity, growth, and yes, a decent amount of venture capital. So, go ahead, dazzle them—but also make them think. Because in the high-stakes game of venture capital, a well-executed product slide could very well be your royal flush.

## The team slide

In early stage startups, the team slide is, by quite some considerable margin, the most important slide. 

You can probably think of the team slide as the startup world’s equivalent of a reality TV show; it’s where investors decide whether you and your motley crew are "The A-Team," "Survivor" material, or closer to the cast of a low-budget soap opera. Your team slide is so important that it alone can tip the balance between you securing millions in funding or parting with an awkward handshake and a thank you for the coffee. 

If your startup were a space mission, your team would be the crew piloting the ship. Investors want to know if you have the right people to not only launch but also land safely with a payload of profits. The narrative you spin around your team is so critical that it could make the difference between "Houston, we have funding," and "Houston, we have a problem."

### **What are investors looking for?**

The key phrase here is founder-market fit. 

Investors are backing you and your founding team more than anything else when they put their money into a startup. They need to be certain that you’re the right person, leading the right team, to accomplish your mission. What it comes down to is that investors are essentially risk-minimizers in disguise. 

A great startup team is a de-facto competitive advantage for your company. This is the team that’s going to build the category-defining future for your industry. Do you have what it takes? Do you have the right blend of technical and business skills to make it happen? 

So, what they're looking for, besides your ability to give a solid high-five, is:

  1. **Previous startup experience** : Investors love serial entrepreneurs, even those who've watched their previous ventures crash and burn. Why? Because those battle scars are lessons learned.

  2. **Successful exits** : If you've managed to sell off a company or go public, you're essentially the LeBron James of startups.

  3. **Special skills and expertise** : Perhaps you have a PhD in artificial intelligence, or you're an expert in the organic avocado toast industry. Highlight it: Industry expertise and the Rolodex to go with it is gold dust to investors. 

  4. **Are you an expert customer?** : Sometimes, the best person to solve a problem is someone who's experienced it firsthand. If you've lived the problem you're solving, say it loud and proud.

  5. **Founding team focus** : Who has skin in the game? Employees may come and go like seasons of a TV series, but the founding team is the mainstay. These are your fellow hobbits on a quest to Mordor.

  6. **Team composition** : It helps to give a quick breakdown of the team's expertise, like "90% are developers who can code in their sleep, and the other 10% are customer support heroes."


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

If your team is as exciting as you believe, then telling its story should be as effortless. 

Was your CTO part of a successful Silicon Valley exit? That's your "Mission: Impossible" stunt scene. Did your COO turn around a failing company? That's your drama-filled climax. Weave these stories into a cohesive, gripping narrative that ends with your team standing triumphantly on a metaphorical mountain peak.

Tie all past experience to this company - and focus on the ways that your founding team is a perfect gang of humans to build this specific company. 

### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Embrace weaknesses** : Every Superman has his Kryptonite. Acknowledge it and show how other team members fill those gaps.

  2. **The right fit** : Make sure to communicate why each team member is perfect for their role, like puzzle pieces in a jigsaw.


### **Common pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: LinkedIn overload 

We get it. Your CTO once interned at SpaceX, your COO has a black belt in Six Sigma, and your VP of Sales is a former Navy SEAL. However, cluttering the slide with every LinkedIn achievement is like filling your living room with trophies: impressive, but ultimately overwhelming. 

Stick to the highlights that directly apply to your startup’s needs. Are you a tech startup? Sure, bring up that SpaceX internship. Otherwise, keep it streamlined, like a sports car, not a lumbering RV.

Pitfall 2: You’re missing your Jedi Master

Let’s face it: you’re the Luke Skywalker of this startup galaxy—bright-eyed, bushy-tailed, and filled with potential. But where’s your Obi-Wan? It’s crucial to have at least one team member with a proven track record in the field or adjacent industry. This not only boosts your credibility but offers comfort to investors, proving you have at least one hand on deck that has successfully navigated the asteroid field of startups before.

Pitfall 3: The “too many cooks” phenomenon 

You understand intimately the value that everyone in your team brings to your project, which makes you want to introduce everyone, from the visionary (that's usually you) to the intern who makes killer coffee. 

The problem is, while that’s charming, it’s distracting. Feature only the key players—those whose absence would fundamentally alter your venture’s fate. Think of it as being the headliners before the movie, not the entire cast and crew that rolls when it’s finished.

Pitfall 4: There’s a personality void 

Investors invest in people as much as they do in ideas. A team slide that reads like a grocery list of skills and qualifications, devoid of any personality or spirit, is a missed opportunity. Add a touch of flair that conveys your team’s culture or dynamic. Are you the ‘scrappy underdogs’? The ‘data-driven dynamos’? Give them a sense of not just who you are but how you function as a unit.

Pitfall 5: Overlooking the role of your advisors 

You may think of the advisors as the ‘extras’ in your feature film, but in reality, they’re more like those character actors whose names you can't remember but whose faces assure you that the movie you’re watching probably isn’t a total waste of time. 

If you’ve secured advisors with significant industry clout, don’t banish them to the ‘Special Thanks’ section. Feature them, but don't let them steal the limelight from your core team.

Big warning: I often see advisors listed who don’t even remember your company. If an investor recognizes an advisor on your slide, you’d best believe they’ll ping off a text or a call to see how involved they are. If they go ‘Billy _who?_ Oh yeah, I spoke to them at a conference once,’ that’s game over: you’ve been caught in what’s essentially a lie. There’s no coming back from that. 

### **Summary**

So there it is. Your team slide is not just a PowerPoint slide; it's a microcosm of your startup's universe. It's the Avengers Assemble moment that could lead to world-saving solutions or at least some pretty nifty gadgets. Remember, you’re not just pitching a product or a service; you’re pitching a group of human beings capable of turning a vision into reality, which makes it worth its weight in term sheets.

## Market size slide

You need to think of your market size slide as being the ticket that offers reassurance to your investors that they are about to board a luxury yacht bound for Profit Island, not a dinky rowing boat that might well be holey. 

With this slide you're not just saying, "Trust me, this is a good idea." You're saying, "Trust me, this is a good idea that can and will make us obscenely rich."

You need this slide because even the most captivating narrative or ingenious product will generate yawns if you can't convincingly show there's a real, growing, and, importantly, lucrative market for it. Imagine being a shepherd with the world's fluffiest, most charming sheep but no one to sell wool to. Bummer, right? This slide is where you show the world that people want your wool, and they want it bad.

## Make sure you know the difference between top-down and bottom-up market sizing

Top-down and bottom-up market sizing are two different approaches used to estimate the size of a market or the potential revenue that can be generated within a specific industry or segment. Each approach has its advantages and limitations, and they can be used individually or in combination for a more comprehensive analysis. Here's a description of the key differences between these two methods:

  1. **Top-Down Market Sizing** : In the top-down approach, market size is estimated by starting with a broad view of the entire market and then drilling down to estimate the target segment's share. It typically relies on external data sources, industry reports, and macroeconomic data.

     * **Data Sources** : Top-down analysis often uses data from government agencies, industry associations, market research reports, and existing industry knowledge.

     * **Process** : The process begins with obtaining data on the total addressable market (TAM), which represents the entire market's size. Researchers then make assumptions or use available data to estimate the market share or penetration that the target segment can capture.

     * **Use Cases** : Top-down analysis is useful when you need a quick estimate of the market size or when detailed data about a specific segment is scarce. It's also often used for high-level strategic planning.

     * **Limitations** : One of the main limitations is that it can provide only a rough estimate and may not accurately capture the nuances of specific segments. Assumptions made at the top level can introduce errors, and the analysis may lack granularity.

  2. **Bottom-Up Market Sizing** : The bottom-up approach, on the other hand, starts with individual data points and builds up to estimate the overall market size. It is a more detailed and granular approach.

     * **Data Sources** : Bottom-up analysis relies on primary data sources, such as surveys, interviews, customer data, and other specific information about the target market or customer base.

     * **Process** : Researchers collect data at the ground level, often starting with a small sample or a specific geographic area. They then extrapolate or scale up the findings to estimate the market size as a whole.

     * **Use Cases** : Bottom-up analysis is valuable when you have access to detailed data about a specific segment or when you need a more accurate and granular market size estimate. It is commonly used for business planning, product development, and investment decisions.

     * **Limitations** : Bottom-up analysis can be time-consuming and resource-intensive, especially if comprehensive data collection is required. Additionally, it may not account for market dynamics or changes at the macroeconomic level.


In practice, a combination of top-down and bottom-up approaches is often used to triangulate market size estimates. This helps mitigate the limitations of each method and provides a more robust and reliable estimate of market potential. 

However, using both can be very confusing if you’re trying to put together a concise story - present one, then use the other approach for a sense-check. 

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are looking for the Three T's: **Total, Trajectory, and Target**.

  1. **Total market size** : How big is the cake we're talking about? 

  2. **Trajectory** : Is the market growing? Is the cake getting bigger each year? 

  3. **Target market** : Who is going to eat all of this cake? 


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

Numbers, while essential, can be as dry as a sandpaper smoothie if you don’t bring them to life somehow. Use storytelling! 

Introduce a potential customer—let's call her Sally. Explain how Sally represents a multitude in desperate need of your product. "Imagine Sally, multiplied by 1 million, all yearning for the relief only our product can bring!"

### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **TAM, SAM, and SOM** : These acronyms aren't mystical chants; they are the cornerstones of top-down market sizing. 

     * **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** : This is the size of the full pie, like the total sales of toothpaste in a country.

     * **SAM (Serviceable Available Market)** : Now, what if your toothpaste is for vegan, free-spirited, yoga-loving seniors? That's your SAM - not everyone is going to want your specific product, after all. 

     * **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)** : How many vegan, yoga-practising seniors can you sell your toothpaste to within a 10-mile radius of your factory, within a reasonable timeframe?

  2. **Trajectory** : It's not enough that there's a market; that market has to be growing, or at least have the potential to grow.


### **Common Pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: The “inflated universe” technique 

This is the sorcery of numbers, where the entrepreneur brandishes a figure so gigantic it might as well be the budget for a Hollywood blockbuster or the national debt. "$500 billion!," you proclaim, like Dr Evil in Austin Powers. But wait, is this a market you can realistically access, or is it more like counting the stars in the galaxy when you're selling telescopes? Make sure your market size is realistic, not astronomical.

Pitfall 2: The vagueness conundrum 

"We're targeting social media users," you say, waving a wand as if you're summoning the entire populace of Twitter and Instagram. While this may sound like a broad enough market, it's about as clear as mud. Being vague about your target market leaves investors skeptical about whether you know who you're selling to. That's like saying you're selling to "people who breathe." Get specific!

Pitfall 3: The “bottom-up” illusion 

This is the classic case of extrapolating that if each person in New York City buys your artisanal, gluten-free bagel, you'll be a billionaire in no time. It's called the 'bottom-up' approach, but let's be frank, it often bottoms out because it assumes zero competition and 100% market capture. Ground your numbers in reality, or your dreams of market domination might just become a lead balloon.

Now, there _are_ times where doing bottom-up market sizing makes sense. That’s usually only true if you are genuinely creating a brand new market that didn’t start before. Let’s be honest, though: That’s rare enough to be virtually non-existent. In almost all cases, you’ll want a top-down market sizing. 

Pitfall 4: Ignoring the competitive landscape 

You've done your homework, and you're sure your market is larger than the landmass of three small European countries combined. Splendid! But did you remember to acknowledge the armies already occupying the territory? Ignoring the competitive landscape is akin to planning a picnic in a jungle full of predators. Explain how you'll differentiate or compete effectively in the market you've defined. If you don't, you might as well serve your venture up on a silver platter to the competition.

Pitfall 5: The anachronistic trap 

You're proudly proclaiming that your market is growing 20% year-over-year, and everyone should hop onto this runaway gravy train. One minor hiccup: Your data is as outdated as a mullet in a fashion show. Fresh data isn't just a garnish; it's the main course. Make sure your figures are as recent as possible and clearly cited.

### **Conclusion**

In a nutshell, the market size slide is where you establish that the world isn't just ready for your product, it's downright thirsty for it. This is where you show that you're not just peddling magic beans, but beans that will grow into a stalk that leads to a goose that lays golden eggs in a market full of people screaming, "We want golden eggs and we want them now!"

Investors are not just buying into a product or a team; they're buying into a story of growth and prosperity. This slide is where you lay down the roadmap to that prosperity and reassure your investors that their seat on this exciting journey is first class. So make it compelling, make it robust, and make it believable. Because, in the end, everyone wants to believe that they're investing in the next big thing. And this slide is where you make the case that they are.

## Competition slide

Your competition slide works hand-in-glove with your [market slide](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/109877/lessons/367370>). The market slide illustrates the people you’ve anticipated will purchase your product—or at least, the size of the pie that represents how many people might be interested in doing so. 

The competition slide explores who else is trying to solve the same problem as you and will also be angling themselves at those same potential customers. If you’re going to make your business fly, you need a comprehensive understanding of who and what you’re up against, which makes your competition slide crucial.

Let’s get something out of the way early doors. You have competition. You always have competition. If you don’t have competition, then the chances are that you’re trying to solve a problem that doesn’t actually exist. So take a deep breath, take a step back, and start to analyse your business space objectively. 

When I say that you definitely have competition, and you’re a bit flummoxed by this because there’s noone else out there building what you are, how you’re doing it, think of it this way: your competition doesn’t have to be direct and obvious. You know what Netflix’s biggest competitor is? Nope, not the cinema. It’s not even Disney+ or Apple TV. It’s sleep. That’s right, at some point Netflix realized that the choice isn’t between watching another episode on Netflix or another channel—it is between continuing your binge, or tucking in for the night. 

## Identifying your competitors

What you need to ask yourself when it comes to identifying your competition is ‘What is the problem that I’m solving, and what means are my customers currently using to do this?’ The first, rudimentary, hand-cranked dishwasher was invented in 1850 but dishwashers closer to how we understand them first came about in 1886, because Josephine Cochrane was fed up with her staff dropping and breaking her expensive crockery. That was the first dishwasher’s competition: a scullery maid. 

Now, we have dishwasher manufacturers such as Miele and AEG competing on efficiency and environmental credentials, but they are also up against people who do their dishes by hand and (heaven help the environment) those who have given up on dishes altogether and use single-use, disposable items. 

Your competition might not be obvious, but it definitely exists, and it deserves your full attention. 

## What are investors looking for?

  1. **Differentiation** : Your investors need to be able to see that you’re doing something different from your competitors. This might include your solution being:

     1. Cheaper

     2. Faster

     3. More efficient or productive

     4. More pleasant to use

     5. Or just… Different in some way

  2. **Understanding** : You need to know your competitors inside-out, and back-to-front as well. This will include:

     1. How do they acquire their customers?

     2. How do they serve their customers?

     3. Are their customers happy?


## How do you tell this part of the story?

When it comes to comparing yourself to your competitors, the key thing to remember is that you’re not just pitting their product against yours. You’re also showing how your companies are both similar and different. So that will include things like customer service, which can make a huge difference to your customers.

There are different formats that you can use to present this information. One is in a table, where you set out how your company compares to the competition on different criteria. 

Or you can position yourself on a two-axis graph, with you in the top right. The variables that you include on the axes will depend on the strengths and weaknesses of your company in its market sector. But it might, for example, indicate that you offer the best value for money and the highest grade of efficiency. 

## What do you need to remember?

  1. **You can never tell the full story** : There will be too much information to convey on one slide. But that’s okay. What’s more important is that you can spark a good conversation and provide a jumping-off point for your investors’ due diligence research.

  2. **Investors might know of competitors you don’t mention** : Maybe you don’t mention them because they didn’t fall within your selection criteria. Or perhaps you just haven’t noticed them. If it’s the first situation, you need to be clear on why some competitors were included and others were omitted. If it’s the second issue, then admit to it. Don’t lie! Make a note of the name, do some research, and then email the investor with what you discovered and how it fits into your landscape. 


## Common pitfalls

Pitfall 1: We don’t have any competition!

Yes, we’ve already covered this. But it’s worth reiterating. If you don’t think you have any competition you’re either underestimating the market or you’re not actually solving a problem that’s in need of a solution. 

Pitfall 2: Dismissing the competition

Avoid talking dismissively or contemptuously about your competitors. Apart from it setting a disrespectful culture among your company, it looks poor to investors who will be wary of your arrogance and lack of both self-awareness and market-awareness. And what if it’s an incumbent that eventually buys your company? That doesn’t exactly suggest a comfortable working environment.

Pitfall 3: Following the herd

Don’t be overly influenced by the competition. You’re in this game to try to change how things are done, not to follow the herd. You might be able to see how they do things, but their why could be more obscure. March to your own drum: it’s a powerful beat. 

## Summary

If you’re doing this right, then you’ll know more about your competition than will comfortably fit in the entire deck, let alone on one slide. And most of that will not come up in conversation, either. But that’s just fine. You need to understand everything about your problem, solution, and the market it sits in, which includes the competition. It’s all to your benefit, even if you don’t get to show it off.

## The traction slide

Traction is a must-have metric for you. However, knowing what you need to share so that you’ll be taken seriously can be pretty tricky. It’s all too easy to get caught up in hype—that’s your own and other’s—and throw that around, rather than anything of substance, or to panic that you’ve nothing to show for your efforts and fail to include anything at all. 

Your traction slide serves as the concrete evidence that you’re not just playing startup; you're actually pulling it off. And even if you don’t think you’ve got anything you can show as traction, the chances are you have. Here’s what you need to show.

Traction is the universe's subtle way of saying, "Well, look who's getting things right!" It's not merely your revenue: it’s a measure of your business's gravitational pull, how it attracts customers, revenue, partnerships, and sometimes even adoration. Traction is essentially the metrics or KPIs (Key Performance Indicators) that suggest that there’s a there there in your company.

### **Why do you need this slide?**

Why does a peacock have feathers? To show off, of course! But also to attract a mate—in your case, an investor mate willing to open their checkbook. This slide gives those with money a reason to be less protective of it. It demonstrates to them that you’re getting something right and with a bit of a push—that being their money—it can get even more right.

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are the Sherlock Holmes of the financial world, forever sniffing around for clues of success or failure. Sure, revenue is the diamond tiara of all metrics, but investors dig deeper. Other metrics that they’ll look for include:

  1. **Lifetime value of a customer (LTV):** How much is a customer worth over the entire duration of their relationship with your business?

  2. **Cost of acquiring a customer (CAC)** : What’s the price tag for wooing a customer? If you’re spending a king's ransom to purchase infertile land, we’ve got a problem.

  3. **Monthly active users (MAU)** : It's not just about who's tried your product; it’s about who's sticking around, using it actively.

  4. **Milestones** : Not raking in the bucks yet? Milestones, like hiring a key team member or completing a successful pilot, can act as your "proxy traction."


### **What do you do if you don’t have revenue yet?**

You need a traction slide no matter what, but if you don’t have revenue yet, you have to get creative. Think of traction as all the things you’ve done to make your business less risky.

This is common enough that we’re [covering that in a separate section](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/knowledge-base/sections/120565/lessons/406190>)… 

### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

The traction part of your pitch needs to be intricate, data-driven, and sprinkled with moments of triumph. Use charts that go up and to the right, but only if they honestly do so. Share customer testimonials or anecdotes that put flesh on your numerical skeleton.

### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Find your metric** : A viable traction metric is as crucial to an investor as a compass is to a lost hiker. The sooner you identify and start tracking it, the better your pitch will fare.

  2. **Be transparent** : Always make sure that your data can withstand the scrutiny of a potential investor's due diligence. No fudging the numbers because you’ll be found out.


### **Common pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: The mirage of vanity metrics 

Vanity metrics are dazzling, sparkly numbers that look good on the surface but are as meaningful as a pet rock's life ambitions. 

100,000 downloads sound impressive until one realizes that 99% of those users opened the app once and then left it to gather digital dust. In the immortal words of what someone somewhere probably once said: "Not all that glitters is user engagement." Stick to metrics that matter and demonstrate real, sustainable growth or engagement.

Pitfall 2: The “we're killing it in Estonia” trap 

So your app is a big hit in Estonia, or your automated garden gnome is flying off the shelves in a small pocket of rural Saskatchewan. While geographical success isn't to be scoffed at, it's crucial to frame these accomplishments in a broader context. 

If your product is resonating in a niche market, make sure you can articulate why and how this success is transferable to larger, more lucrative arenas. Otherwise, investors may label you a one-hit-wonder, doomed to serenade Estonians for all eternity.

Pitfall 3: The line graph of delusion 

Exponential growth line graphs; they are the venture capitalist's siren song. If you've had three good months, it can be tempting to extrapolate that into an eternity of up-and-to-the-right bliss. Yet, the savvy investor will see through your time-bending charade and ask what's driving those projections. Avoid the over-optimistic hockey stick graph that assumes everyone in the Milky Way will be using your cat yoga app by 2030. Keep it real, keep it credible.

Pitfall 4: It's coming, honestly!

The traction slide is about what you've achieved, not what you hope to do once you've bought that fancier espresso machine and hired an intern named Chad. Promising big partnerships, name-dropping industry titans, or asserting that any day now you'll break through—all without concrete evidence—is akin to stating you're just one lottery ticket away from being a millionaire. Stick to the present facts, not future fiction.

Pitfall 5: The scattergun approach 

When it comes to displaying traction, less can often be more. Imagine presenting a slide so chock-full of numbers, percentages, and acronyms that it resembles the control panel of a malfunctioning spaceship. Your audience will spend their time untangling the web of data rather than focusing on your indisputably brilliant product. Choose a handful of key metrics that tell a compelling story and go with those.

### **Summary**

In summary, your traction slide is where you get to strut your stuff. It's the evidence that your startup isn't just sound and fury, signifying nothing. When done right, this slide can be the lighthouse that guides investors around the rocky shores of your up-and-coming enterprise. So make it factual, make it compelling, and most of all, make it irrefutably about your growing success.

## The pricing & business model slide

You might want to think of your business model and pricing slide as the gritty police procedural in your pitch deck's prime-time lineup. It's not always the flashiest, but when done well, it hooks you in and makes you care about the characters, or numbers, in this case. 

Your business model is where your grandiose tale of disruption and innovation meets the merciless real world of spreadsheets and calculators. Let's delve into its nuances, shall we?

Imagine a sci-fi film where the hero has the universe’s most potent weapon but no idea how to turn it on. That's your startup without a business model. However, if you're so nascent that your business model is more theory than fact, you could sneak this slide into the appendix. The slide spells out how you plan to turn innovation into cold, hard cash (or cryptocurrency, if that’s your jam).

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors crave metrics like gardeners crave compost: unsightly but essential. So, give them the goods. We're talking:

  1. **COGS (cost of goods sold)** : Important if you’re making things that actually, you know, exist in three dimensions.

  2. **CAC (customer acquisition cost)** : If you're spending more on Google Ads than you'd earn in six lifetimes, that’s a problem.

  3. **LTV (lifetime value)** : If your customer stays around longer than a carton of milk in the fridge, you might be onto something.

  4. **R &D costs**: Only important if your R&D costs more than a small country’s GDP. In that case, you’ll need to justify it.

  5. **Pricing model** : If you're the first SaaS company to charge customers in jars of homemade jam, investors will want to know.


### **What types of business models are there?**

  1. **One-off sales** : Simple. You sell, they buy. Think artisanal beard combs.

  2. **Subscription model** : The gift that keeps on taking (from the customer's bank account but keeps giving to yours). Example: Netflix.

  3. **Hybrid model** : Like a platypus, a bit of this and a bit of that. This could be a setup fee + subscription, or a hardware purchase + subscription type model.

  4. **Freemium model** : The first taste is free; after that, you pay. Spotify has mastered this art.

  5. **Advertising model** : You're not the customer; you're the product. Facebook waves from this camp.

  6. **Licensing** : You develop patents, and others pay you. Not just for patent trolls, it turns out. 

  7. **Franchising** : A specific subset of licencing—hello Ace Hardware, Burger King, and many others.

  8. **Others** : There’s a _lot_ of potential business models out there—can’t cover ‘em all here—and it’s likely that you’re a specialist in your market anyway. 


Don’t fall for the temptation to have more than one business model if you can help it. A startup with too many business models is like a dog chasing two rabbits: it's twice as frenzied and half as effective. The allure of diversified income may shimmer like a mirage, but remember, you're not a multinational conglomerate. 

You're a scrappy early-stage startup, with limited resources and a team that still debates over whether to buy the fancy coffee. Stick to mastering one model, lest you spread yourself too thin and master none, and make sure that if you decide to ditch one business model in favor of another you’ve done it having given it every possible chance to succeed. In business, as in comedy, timing and focus are everything.

### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

Keep it simple. Your business model is probably not even a separate slide, to be honest. You can probably summarize it to something like “B2B SaaS, with pricing ranging from $15 to $50 per seat, depending on feature set”. 

### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Optional, but not really** : A business model slide can be relegated to the appendix, but.. That’s a red flag in itself. Even if it’s obvious, confirm the obvious, and move on. 

  2. **Novelty sells** : If your pricing model is as groundbreaking as the first iPhone, then for the love of all things holy and profitable, include it in your main pitch.

### **Common pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: “If we build it, they will come”

Please don’t get caught up in the naïve notion that the sheer brilliance of your idea will naturally beckon customers like moths to a flame with no marketing budget required. 

Unfortunately, reality is not a Kevin Costner movie. Including a business model devoid of a go-to-market strategy is like composing a symphony and never bothering to hire an orchestra. Without concrete plans for customer acquisition and retention, you might as well be trying to sell ice to penguins.

Pitfall 2: Not having a path to convert users to revenue 

Virality doesn’t stand alone. You present a chart where the X-axis represents time and the Y-axis represents users, and both are shooting skywards in a manner most pleasing. But where, oh where, is the revenue? 

Capturing users is all well and good, but if your business model doesn't show a clear path from user to revenue, you're essentially running a very expensive hobby. Remember: Monopoly money is not accepted in the real world.

Pitfall 3: Too many freebies 

Free trials! Free tiers! Free puppies with every subscription! While offering something for free might attract initial interest, it could also drive you into a fiscal cul-de-sac. 

Making everything free and then vaguely waving at "monetization" as a future event is a bit like setting sail for a treasure island without a map, compass, or even a working knowledge of what treasure looks like. Don't go bankrupt by being too generous.

Except the free puppies. Everyone loves those. Definitely go all-in on free puppies. 

Pitfall 4: The delusion of “our only expense is coffee” 

Underestimating costs is as common as finding cat videos on the internet—and equally as dangerous (to your business, not the cats). 

Whether it's forgetting to account for overheads, salaries, or the inevitable unexpected setbacks (because there will be setbacks), low-balling your expenses will get you raised eyebrows rather than raised capital. Make sure to present a full, unvarnished picture of your expected cash outflows.

Pitfall 5: The “don’t worry about it” revenue model 

This is the mirror image of Pitfall 4, where the entrepreneur is overly optimistic about the revenue streams but gets sketchy about the details. 

Telling your investors that you’ll make money through “synergies, economies of scale, and other buzzwords” is about as comforting as a lullaby sung by a chainsaw. Outline clear, actionable revenue streams and back them up with data, forecasts, or—dare we dream—even some initial sales.

### **Summary**

To sum up, the business model and pricing slide is the vital, dependable, detail in your startup. It might not make headlines, but without it, there's no story. So make it compelling, make it insightful, but above all, make it make sense. Because if you don’t, not only will the investors swipe left, but you might also find yourself questioning what you’re doing in the first place. And existential crises are generally best saved for after the Series A round.

## The ask and use of funds slide

There’s no two ways about it, the ask slide is where you unabashedly shout, "Show me the money!" But you don't just want _any_ money. 

You want a specific amount of money for specific things. You're not a pirate hastily burying treasure; you're an entrepreneur with a plan. 

Heads up: This is, by quite some considerable margin, the slide where the vast majority of founders go wrong. Pay attention—it’s crucial. 

I’m going to be blunt: this is not the slide to be coy. If you don't ask, you don't get. It's like going to a restaurant and hoping the chef guesses what you want. 

### **What are investors looking for?**

  1. **Specific sum** : Not "around a million or so." Say, "$1,250,000." It shows you've done your homework.

  2. **Clear use of funds** : A broad 'for business stuff' won't cut it. Break it down.

  3. **Milestones attached** : The use of funds should ideally be tied to specific milestones. It’s like GPS for your investor's money.


### **How to tell this part of the story?**

You need to have goals and they need to be SMART. That means specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound. You also need these goals to be waymarkers to your next fundraising round. Here’s how it might look:

  1. **Team** : "By March 2024, we will have hired a squad of world-dominating engineers and marketers."

  2. **Development** : "By June 2024, we will launch our product that will make toasters as obsolete as the typewriter."

  3. **Growth** : "By September 2024, we will have more users than there are stars in our galaxy (okay, maybe just 2,000, but still)."


### **The financial calculus**

Work out the costs of these milestones, add a buffer for when life inevitably laughs at your plans (30%-70% is a safe bet), and boom: you have an ask. It's not a number you scribbled on a napkin; it’s a thoroughly researched, meticulously calculated figure.

### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Be crystal clear** : If your ask is as vague as a fortune cookie, you're doing it wrong. Know how much money you need to get to your next fundraising round.

  2. **Link to next fundraising** : Make sure your milestones are geared towards goals that will make your next fundraising round easier. "Here's what we'll have achieved by the time we meet again, hats in hands." 


### **Common pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: "Let's make it rain!"

There’s a certain glamor that comes with having cash in the bank. Like a teenager with their first paycheck, some founders get starry-eyed and list extravagant costs. "Yes, we need a corporate yacht—how else would we brainstorm effectively?" 

Beware, this doesn't say "I'm a visionary"; it screams, "I'm a liability!" Itemize your needs meticulously and stick to what truly accelerates your business plan. If your expenses look like a rock star's backstage demands, you've probably gone astray.

Pitfall 2: The nebulous abyss of “miscellaneous”

"Miscellaneous" is the black hole of budgeting. A small allocation for unexpected costs? Reasonable. An enormous chunk of your funds earmarked for "Miscellaneous" or "Other"? Red flag! 

Investors don't want their money to vanish into a financial Bermuda Triangle. Be as specific as possible. If you can't explain it, neither will the venture capitalists' spreadsheets.

Pitfall 3: "Trust me, I’m an innovator" vagueness 

It's not enough to say, "We'll use the funds for R&D." What exactly are you developing? A teleporter? Self-buttering toast? Investors want to know where their money is going—not just the department, but the project, the goal, and the metrics for success. 

Bring them into your world, but don't make them guess what's going on there. This isn't a murder mystery dinner; it's a business proposal.

Pitfall 4: Overlooking that you need a revenue model 

You'd be surprised how often this happens. Entrepreneurs get so caught up in the "How we'll spend it" that they forget the "How we'll make more of it." 

If your use of funds slide is all outflow and no inflow, you've essentially laid out a plan for a very expensive party that ends in bankruptcy. Remember, the aim isn't just to spend money, but to use it to generate more–at some point. If that doesn’t happen in the time horizon of _this_ fundraise, then paint a picture of how what you’re doing now makes sense in the context of figuring out the fundraising for the next phase of the biz. 

Pitfall 5: "Sudden expenses" suddenness 

Growing is expensive. Parents know this, and the principle is similar for businesses. So you did remember to budget for scaling, didn’t you? Scaling costs, from staff to technology, can be like gremlins—they multiply when you're not looking. Failing to account for these can spell doom for your startup and earn you a quick "No, thank you" from investors.

### **Summary**

In sum, your ask slide is where dreams get real. It’s not the time for abstraction or modesty. It’s the moment of bold, specific, quantified audacity. This is when you spell out your vision in dollars and cents, and if done correctly, it's where investors will begin to see their own futures aligning with yours. So go ahead, ask—and make it a question that’s too compelling to ignore.

## The operating plan slide

The words ‘operating plan’ might sound terrifying, but you really cannot put together a pitch deck without one. It sets out what you plan to accomplish over the next 18 months or so, in numbers. Think of it as a budget, if you like—but in a broader sense. This isn’t just about money, it’s also about major product and company milestones. 

The operating plan is the slide where you show you're not just a dreamer but also a doer. It is where you strip down your grand ambitions to their numerical core. You might say it's where the rubber meets the road, assuming that road is a complicated Excel sheet and the rubber is your startup’s short-term future. 

In essence, it's a snapshot of your company’s goals and strategies transformed into numbers for a future that's hopefully more "Series A" and less "Game Over."

Why numbers, you ask? Well, as much as venture capitalists like to think of themselves as rock stars of finance, they're really number crunchers in designer clothes. Their love language is ROI, KPI, and all the other acronyms that spell "money." So, if you want them to swipe right on your startup, you need to speak in numbers.

### **What goes into this slide**

First, it’s crucial to understand the milestones that will allow you to gallivant off to your next fundraising adventure. 

Say you're eyeing a Series A; what are the achievement badges you need to collect first? Maybe it's 25,000 paying users or a lucrative partnership. These milestones get translated into numbers, providing you with a numerical narrative that can make even a venture capitalist’s heart flutter.

The usual time frame for this slide is an optimistic yet calculated 18 months into the future. You'll want to include major milestones like product launches, partnerships signed, and perhaps something about saving the world if that’s your company's jam. But remember, these aren’t just numbers; they are a story, each figure a chapter heading leading toward your funding denouement.

### **The fine art of presentation**

Don’t just chuck all your digits onto this slide like they're numbers in a lottery. Make it easily digestible, like financial baby food. Round those numbers to a reasonable amount; trust me, nobody wants to know that you're planning to spend precisely $49,851 on chai lattes for the team. A simple $50K will do.

The goal here is to minimize the cognitive load for your audience. You don’t want the venture capitalist to mentally tap out while they're still on your operating plan. Remember, you've got only 20 minutes to woo them before Q&A turns your pitch into a courtroom drama.

### **What to look out for**

Internal consistencies

Your plan needs to make sense with itself. If you're planning a 30× growth in customers, but your marketing budget barely budges, it looks like you're planning to defy the laws of business physics. Make sure the various parts of your operating plan fit together like a well-considered jigsaw puzzle.

External consistencies

Make sure the story your operating plan tells is consistent with the industry. I’ve seen business plans where the 5-year projections are greater than the entire GDP of the country the company was targeting. That’s… unlikely at best, impossible at worst, and in any case, you’re making a complete prat of yourself. 

### **Realistic, but not clairvoyant**

As you finalize your operating plan slide, stick a little disclaimer at the bottom. This is your "these-are-projections-and-I'm-not-a-fortune-teller" clause. It's a polite way of saying that while you have a plan, you're not a business oracle and things might veer off-course. Spoiler alert: They will. This is a startup after all. 

### **Summary**

To sum up, the operating plan slide is not just a monotonous requirement—it's the compelling financial story that complements your equally compelling product and vision. It’s the slide that shows you’re serious, you’ve thought things through, and yes, you can count—something that, in the realm of startups and venture capital, counts for a lot.

## The Moat slide

The moat is a rather dramatic name for a slide, but it’s short and easy to say, and presents a good visual of its meaning: This slide is your chance to demonstrate why your startup is not just another fluffy tech duckling paddling in the endless ocean of opportunities but rather a solid, unassailable castle of a business. Let’s see how deep and wide your moat needs to be to defend your business.

Think of your moat as a business defense mechanism—preferably one without medieval arrows and boiling oil. It's what keeps your competition at bay, enabling you to lounge in your metaphorical castle and sip that metaphorical cup of royalty-grade mocha frappé. 

Different businesses have different moats: patented technologies, extraordinary teams, or even the invisible force field of brand reputation. Moats are not necessarily filled with water; sometimes they're filled with patents, hard-to-replicate technology, or a swarm of loyal customers.

### **Why do you need this slide?**

Just as a castle without a moat is basically an oversized bed and breakfast for invading armies, a startup without a moat is a juicy target for better-armed competitors. Not having a moat isn't the end of the world, mind you, but having one makes you a more appealing investment opportunity. Investors much prefer putting their money into castles that are difficult to besiege. Makes sense, right?

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are like truffle pigs. They have a nose for something valuable but not immediately obvious. If you have any of these, then you are well on your way to excavating your defensive boundaries:

  1. **Your team** : If you have three Nobel Laureates, a handful of PhDs, a marketing genius who could sell vegan meat to a lion, and a couple of people who have deep domain expertise in the industry—you're onto something.

  2. **Patents** : If your intellectual property is locked down tighter than Fort Knox, that's your moat.

  3. **Network effect** : The more people use your product, the more valuable it becomes. Facebook didn't build Rome in a day, you know.


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

Telling this part of your tale involves pointing a finger elaborately at that part of your business that's as unattainable to your competitors as the Holy Grail was to, well, almost everyone who went looking for it. Just remember, subtlety is not your friend here. Use bold fonts, exciting visuals, and strong metrics to make your point. Your aim is to make the investors feel FOMO so severe that they’ll want to fund you just to get inside your castle.

### **What do you need to remember?**

Customers don't really care whether you slay the dragon with a sword or a well-lobbed baguette; they just want the dragon gone. Netflix didn’t succeed because it was great at mailing DVDs; it succeeded because it delivered entertainment directly into the cerebral cortex of human boredom.

### **Common pitfalls**

Moats can, unfortunately, be deceptive. Sometimes you think you have a moat when it’s just a puddle, easily jumpable by any competitor with a good running start. They may not offer the same glitz and glam, but they could be cheaper, faster, or just plain more convenient.

For instance, the Channel Tunnel might offer a quick jaunt between England and France, but ferries and flights offer alternatives that are more wallet-friendly or convenient, depending on your starting point and your destination's GPS coordinates. Just as you wouldn't want to find your castle surrounded by a moat that's easily crossable via a discount inflatable raft, you don't want your business to be easily encroached upon by competitors with a step ladder.

Be clear on those patents, too. There are some great advantages, sure, but the patenting process can be lengthy, expensive, and complex. If you’re not budgeting $30-50k per patent, you’re probably not thinking at the right scale. For many startups with limited resources, the costs involved in obtaining and defending a patent can be burdensome. Moreover, just holding a patent doesn't guarantee protection; enforcing it requires additional legal battles which can be costly and time-consuming. In some cases, competitors might find a way around the patent or create a slightly altered version of the product, thus rendering the patent less effective. So: Don’t rely on ‘em exclusively, but having a few protections ready to go can be helpful. 

### **Summary**

In conclusion, the moat slide is your opportunity to unveil your business's secret sauce, the Colonel's 11 herbs and spices, the magic wand wave that makes you uniquely you—and uniquely unassailable. Make it count, and may your moat be ever filled with whatever it is that keeps competitors at bay: be it patents, talent, or simply the sheer charm of your PowerPoint wizardry.

## The Go-to-market slide

Your go-to-market slide is the point in your pitch where you transition from creating your pain-relieving solution to actually getting it out to the people who need it, and importantly, will pay for it. If that sounds as if it’s a huge reality check, yes, yes it is. And this reality check includes numbers as well.

### **Why do you need this slide?**

You might have solved a hugely pressing problem for a significant portion of the population with a product that they simply can’t live without. But until those people who need it—your target market—know that it exists and recognise how it will change their lives—that’s the value proposition—you’re not going to make any money. To get your product into their lives, you need a go-to-market strategy. There are two unfortunate points about a go-to-market strategy. The first is that it will cost you money. The second is that this cost is referred to as customer acquisition cost, or CAC. Yes, really. 

As well as showing how you’re going to get your product to market, you need to demonstrate that you can manage this at scale, to bring in masses of customers—not just a few—and fast.

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are after a thrilling trilogy here:

  1. **Who are you targeting and how** : Are you courting millennials with an aversion to cooking, or retirees who've suddenly discovered TikTok? This is your target market, or the audience.

  2. **Cost of acquiring a customer (CAC)** : This is the cost of the marketing to get, for example, an audience into the cinema. Posters. Ads. And getting the movie’s stars to do interviews.

  3. **Scale** : Or in cinematic terms, are you a one-hit-wonder or the next Marvel Cinematic Universe? How will the CAC change as you expand? Investors prefer sequels, you see.


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

In your go-to-market slide, you're describing what’s referred to as your 'beachhead.' No, it's not a vacation spot. It's your jumping-off point into the market—it’s the sandbank where you can draw up your boat, leap down onto the beach, and launch yourself into the market.

  1. **Who will you target** : Your target audience isn't "everyone with a pulse." Be specific. 

  2. **Who will you target _first_** : A phased roll-out is commonly a good move for startups. Where can you get foothold first, before you expand your go-to-market approach? Do you segment by customer segment, by geography, or by some other factor? Explain!

  3. **How will you reach them** : Whether it's social media ads or sacrificing a sock to the gods of viral marketing, be clear and specific.


### **What do you need to remember?**

Here's where the technical fine print appears. The go-to-market strategy in your main deck should be different from the one in your appendix.

Main deck

Showcase the Blended CAC. This is the cocktail of all your acquisition costs shaken, not stirred, and divided by the number of customers you've successfully wooed. It's the average you pay to introduce all of your customers to your product.

Appendix

Your appendix is where you get to cover elements of your pitch in a bit more detail. If you have a detailed go-to-market strategy, whack it in the appendix and be prepared to nerd out about it when challenged. When it comes to your go-to-market strategy, break down your CAC by channel: 

  1. **Influencer marketing** : Because nothing says ‘trust’ like a paid endorsement.

  2. **Advertising** : Good old-fashioned eye-catching ads, from billboards to memes.

  3. **Affiliate marketing** : Your business pals who say, "You scratch my back, I'll scratch yours."

  4. **Organic search and word-of-mouth** : The unicorns of the marketing world, rare but magical.

  5. **Press and PR** : It can work!

### **Common Pitfalls**

Pitfall 1: "If you build it, they will come"

This is the entrepreneurial equivalent of expecting a round of applause simply for existing. Many founders believe that their product is so revolutionary that the mere act of launching will cause a stampede of customers. No marketing, no sales strategy, just pure, unadulterated destiny. Spoiler alert: this is not "Field of Dreams," and you're not Kevin Costner. Detailed customer acquisition and retention strategies are what investors want to see, not hubris.

Pitfall 2: The scattergun approach

Please resist the temptation to try to appeal to _everyone_. "Our target market? Humans. Possibly some advanced primates." This is like fishing with dynamite—you might catch something, but it's all going to be a bit, well, messy. In reality, a well-defined target audience allows for a more focused and effective go-to-market strategy. VCs want to know that you have a laser-like focus on a specific demographic, rather than just throwing things at the wall to see what sticks.

Pitfall 3: Budgeting black holes

Financials are like the Force: they have a light side and a dark side, and they bind the startup universe together. Your go-to-market slide should clearly outline the required budget for customer acquisition, sales, marketing, and everything in between. If the numbers don't add up, or if there's a glaring absence of numbers, you're basically asking investors to take a leap of faith into a financial black hole. Even Star Trek had budgets, people.

Pitfall 4: Buzzword bonanza

Who doesn't love a little "synergy" or "disruptive innovation" in the morning? While these terms can add a sprinkle of pizzazz, relying on them too heavily is like trying to build a castle on a foundation of marshmallows. You may sound modern, but you'll collapse under scrutiny. Investors want substance, not jargon. Think of buzzwords as the seasoning, not the steak.

Pitfall 5: The timeline time warp

You're promising nationwide distribution in a month and global dominance in a year? How delightful! And utterly unbelievable. An unrealistic timeline not only strains credibility but also screams inexperience. A well-thought-out, realistic timeline demonstrates that you understand the challenges ahead and are not planning your SpaceX Mars mission just yet.

### **Summary**

In short, the go-to-market slide is like the Rosetta Stone of your pitch deck. It's where business hieroglyphics meet plain language. It shows that you understand how to translate the possibility of your business into reality for customers. So get out there, build that slide like it's the keystone of your entire venture, and may your CAC be ever in your favor!

## Closing slide

You might want to think of your closing slide as the end credits of your pitch deck. If your pitch presentation were a film, this would be that unexpected teaser at the end that makes the audience gasp and think, "Oh, I can't wait for the sequel." It's your last chance to leave an impression that's stickier than a candy bar melted onto a car seat on a hot summer day.

### **Why do you need this slide?**

The brain, that mysterious lump of gray matter, has something called the "recency effect." It loves to remember the last thing it saw, like the last episode of a Netflix binge or the last slice of pizza. That's why your closing slide needs to be both pretty and punchy. You want it to be memorable for all the right reasons.

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are human and humans like things they can remember, like their own names, passwords, and where they left their car keys. In this final slide, investors are looking for that memorable "aha" moment—a slogan, an image, or a concept that haunts them in the best way possible.

### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

Here's what you need to leave the best impression:

  1. **Company name** : You'd think this would be obvious, but you'd be amazed at how many people think their brand logo is as iconic as the McDonald's arches.

  2. **Tag line/mission** : This is your "Just Do It" or "Think Different." Make it snappy and unforgettable, like a pop song you can't stop humming.

  3. **Contact details** : Here, you go full James Bond by leaving behind your contact info. But instead of a sultry whisper, it's clearly displayed for everyone to see.


### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Keep it simple** : Your audience has just been through a rollercoaster of slides. Don't give them mental whiplash at the end.

  2. **Include something memorable** : Think of this as the after-credits scene that makes people say, "I need to know what happens next!"


### **Common pitfalls**

  1. **Not having one at all** : This is like ending a fireworks show with a wet squib—utterly disappointing.

  2. **Too simple** : While simplicity is key, making it too bare can render it forgettable, like a dish without any seasoning.

  3. **Too enigmatic** : This is not a David Lynch movie. Don't make it so mysterious that they can't figure out what you're about.

  4. **Not reminding your audience clearly enough about what you offer** : You want people to leave thinking about your company, not scratching their heads in bewilderment.


So, there you have it: the guide to crafting the perfect "walk-off home run" of a closing slide. Make it memorable, make it resonant, and, for the love of liquidity events, make it count!

# Part 5: Optional and Extra Slides

## Optional slides and optional information

Think of a pitch deck as an all-singing, all-dancing Swiss Army Knife. There will be some tools on it that everyone is always going to use: the knife and the corkscrew, for example. There will also be some tools on it that are far more specialist and apart from the first time you open them up and wonder “What in all heaven and Earth does this do?”, the chances of you actually using them are slim-to-none. 

Those universal tools are the equivalent of the core slides in a deck: everyone needs them.

The specialist tools are more like the optional and appendix slides in a deck. They are only going to apply to some startups in some circumstances.

### **Why are some slides optional?**

The thing is, every investor is unique, with their own interests and specialisms. And your startup is unique, too, with its own story to tell. Which means that while the basics are universally needed—your team, your product, the market—there are slides that aren’t essential for everyone but could give your pitch some extra "oomph" depending on the climate, the investor with whom you’re speaking, or your startup itself.

Some optional slides show up in some businesses, but not in others. As a general rule, if you get repeated questions about a particular aspect of your business, it might be needed as a slide, either in the core deck as a separate slide, as an aspect of another slide, or as a slide in the appendix.

### **Optional slides and their uses:**

Here’s a few examples I’ve seen used to great effect:

  1. **Customer testimonials** : Yes, you say your product is great, but what does Mrs. Doris from Wisconsin think? Adding quotes or mini-case studies can lend social proof. It’s a bit like having a wingman who vouches for you at the bar. Careful, though: testimonials can tread dangerously close to vanity metrics. Keep it crisp and classy. 

  2. **Technology stack** : Especially useful for investors who can distinguish PHP from an HDTV. This is your chance to show off that you're building your product on something more stable than hopes and dreams.

  3. **Regulatory compliance** : Do you need FDA approval? Are you in a highly regulated space like healthcare, finance, or intergalactic trade? Having a slide about your navigation plan through the bureaucratic cosmos could be a selling point.

  4. **Financial projections beyond 5 years** : This is the 'science fiction' section for the optimists in the room. While most of us can't predict what we're having for dinner next Tuesday, some investors may want to see your 10-year vision. Be careful here; Personally I’m deeply sceptical of 3-year forecasts, never mind 5 or 10-year ones. This could venture into the realm of fairy tales pretty quickly.

  5. **Advisory board** : If you have some industry bigwigs or elder wizards supporting your quest, a slide to name-drop them could add gravitas. But remember, name-dropping is a bit like using cologne. A little can be intriguing, but too much is just... overpowering.

  6. **Intellectual property** : Patents can be a double-edged sword. They're a competitive edge but also a sign that you might have to wrestle with legal eagles down the line. Useful if you're inventing something groundbreaking; redundant if you’re launching a dog-walking app.

  7. **Exit strategy** : No, just don’t. [We cover why you shouldn’t go there in a separate session](<https://haje.pub/pg-exit>).


### **Should you include optional slides?**

The answer is as mysterious as the last page of a choose-your-own-adventure book. If you know your audience well, customizing your pitch deck to their known predilections can be a masterstroke. If you're uncertain, it's like equipping your spaceship for every possible eventuality: You'll be prepared, but your ship may get a bit cluttered.

Remember, it's always better to have these slides prepared and not need them, rather than fumbling when an investor asks about your long-term R&D strategy or why Grandma Edna endorsed your product. Optional slides are your narrative spices; use them judiciously to flavor the investment soup you're cooking up. Too few and you’re bland, too many and you’ve got a culinary disaster. Choose wisely!

## Value Propositions

Your value proposition slide is a fizzing moment of excitement when you explain to all and sundry that what you’re cooking up is going to change and improve their lives in heretofore unexpected ways and they don’t only want it, but they _need_ it. 

A value proposition isn’t about what your product does, it’s about the benefits that it brings to your customer. The value proposition slide is like that one friend at a party who knows exactly how to introduce you so that you don't come off as just "another person with a job," but rather as "the fascinating individual you never knew you needed to meet."

Value propositions should, generally, be benefits driven. That your software detects unsafe behavior on the factory floor isn’t a benefit, it’s a feature. The benefit is the value proposition: saving money and reducing injuries on the factory floor. You don’t help the sales department send more emails, you help the sales department generate more revenue. 

You get the picture. 

### **Why do you need this slide?**

This slide is your "elevator pitch within an elevator pitch." It's your chance to make it abundantly clear why your product doesn't just belong in the market but deserves to be a headliner. If your product were a novel, this slide would be the compelling blurb on the back cover that makes people think, "Hmm, I could forsake binge-watching that new show to read this."

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are like dragons guarding their hoard, only occasionally uncurling their scaly bodies to invest in something that piques their curiosity. They're looking for a value proposition that is:

  1. **Specific** : Be as clear as a just-Windexed glass door about what you're offering.

  2. **Pain-focused** : Or rather, focused on relieving some particular pain. Can your product soothe the fiery itch of a need unmet?

  3. **Unique** : If you're the fifth entrepreneur this month pitching an "innovative" dating app for left-handed people, you better make sure yours has a compelling twist.


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

  1. **Less is more** : One crisp, clear sentence can say more than a verbose paragraph.

  2. **Show and tell** : Use visuals that complement your words. A powerful image alongside a strong statement can leave an indelible impression.

  3. **Numbers speak** : If you have compelling statistics that can bolster your claims, by all means, use them!

  4. **Address the skeptic** : Everyone has an inner skeptic. Preemptively answering a question or concern that the audience might have can make your proposition more robust.


### **What do you need to remember?**

  1. **Focus on the feels** : This slide is less about the nuts and bolts of your product and more about the warm, fuzzy feelings—or exhilarating thrills—it gives the consumer.

  2. **It's not about you** : A common mistake is making your value proposition all about how great your company or product is. Remember, it's not a "We're Awesome!" slide; it's a "You'll Feel Awesome!" slide.


### **Common pitfalls**

  1. **Confusing branding with value proposition** : If your value proposition sounds like it could be on a bumper sticker or a t-shirt, it's probably not a good value proposition. This is not the time for slogans or catchphrases.

  2. **Being too vague or too complex** : Remember, investors are smart but they're not clairvoyant, nor do they have the patience of a saint. Make your value proposition understandable, direct, and compelling.

  3. **Ignoring the competition** : If your value proposition doesn't explain why you're different from the competition, then why should a customer choose you over them?

  4. **Overhyping** : Enthusiasm is good, but overconfidence can be a deal-breaker. Don't promise the moon unless you have a fully-fueled rocket ship to get there.


So there you have it. Crafting the perfect value proposition slide is like serving the perfect cup of coffee—it needs to have the right blend of elements, presented at the right temperature, to wake up your investors and have them savoring the taste long after the presentation is over. And remember, if you manage to nail this, you're not just another face in the crowd; you're the one they'll remember. Now, go out there and be unforgettable.

## The Why Now slide

I have ‘why now’ as one of my crucial checkboxes when I review a pitch deck, but it doesn’t always have to be a separate slide. I want to know why a startup couldn’t have been successful five years ago, and why five years from now is too late. Did something change in regulation, legal frameworks, technology, socio-economically, or otherwise? Name it and leverage it for your pitch. 

### **Why do you need this slide?**

Your pitch needs a sense of urgency. It’s as simple as that. If people were doing this five years ago, what makes your version, in this moment, different and better? If it doesn’t matter whether you do this today or in a year’s time, why should an investor bother putting money into your idea? There are things that can bring more immediate rewards.

Investors aren't just investing in a good idea; they're investing in a good idea whose time has come, to be implemented by you. By outlining why the universe has conspired to make this the opportune moment for your business, you're essentially saying, "Board this train now, or forever hold your peace."

### **What are investors looking for?**

Investors are eager to spot a few things, much like birdwatchers but with fewer binoculars and more spreadsheets:

  1. **The trend you're riding** : Whether it's remote work, ethical consumption, or the return of mullets, what is the big cultural or technological wave that your venture surfs like a pro?

  2. **The timing of the trend** : Your startup is a bit like Cinderella, and the clock is ticking. What has recently changed to make this the perfect moment? It could be:

     * A technological breakthrough, such as AI suddenly becoming good enough to simulate conversations with—ahem—virtual assistants.

     * A market shift, for example a pandemic turning everyone into remote workers.

     * New policies or regulations, much like cannabis legalization or net zero targets opening up new business avenues.


### **How do you tell this part of the story?**

This slide isn't about dry, academic history—it's a recap worthy of a telenovela season finale. So make sure that you include:

  1. **Market potential** : Essentially, you're asking investors to join your caravan on a journey to Eldorado. Show them that the city of gold actually exists and that you've got the best map.

  2. **Trend** : You're not just a ship; you're a ship with a strong wind at its back. Describe the meteorological miracle powering your sails.

  3. **Team strength** : Here's where you say, "Not only do we have a ship and a favorable wind, but we've also got Captain Jack Sparrow at the helm." You know, but in a less piratey way.


### **What do you need to remember?**

This slide is all about surfing, my friend. You're demonstrating that not only can you spot the perfect wave but you've also got the skills to ride it all the way to the beach, where treasure chests of ROI await.

### **Common pitfalls**

Ever heard of the Spinning Jenny? Of course not, you're not a textile history buff. But you do know that early inventions often get their thunder stolen by latecomers who make tiny, brilliant tweaks. Just like Google wasn't the first search engine and Facebook wasn't the first social network, remember, timing is often more crucial than originality.

You see, innovation is not just about being the first to invent the wheel; it's also about being the first to put that wheel on a chariot and ride into battle (or the market). Your "why now" slide should convince investors that your chariot is not only battle-ready but is also hitting the battlefield when the enemy is most vulnerable and the gods of business are smiling down on you.

So, when you stand before that projector, illuminate not just your slides but the light bulbs over investors' heads. Make them realize that this is it: the cosmic conjunction they've been waiting for. And all they need to do is write a check to join the party.

Time waits for no startup. Make sure your audience knows that, too.

## How to think about unit economics

Whether you’re the founder of a startup so young it’s blinking in the daylight or the CEO of a well-established company, it’s incumbent on you to understand your business’s finances, from top to bottom and left to right. You need to know how much you’re making, how much you’re spending, and you need to know the financial building blocks of your company, or its unit economics.

A unit is any basic, quantifiable item that creates value for your company: A product shipped in the mail, a subscriber on a SaaS product, or an hour of time, if you’re billing by the hour. 

The key thing to keep in mind when pitching to VCs, though, is that you’re probably about to go on the accelerator, and things are about to change. 

## What are unit economics?

Unit economics are the direct revenues and costs associated with a particular business model expressed on a per-unit basis. They provide insights into the fundamental profitability of a company's business model by breaking down the company's financials to the smallest possible unit. This could be a single product sold, a customer served, or even an hour of service provided.

## Show how your business becomes more efficient at scale

As a business scales, unit economics can change significantly. For instance, as production volume increases, the cost per unit might decrease due to economies of scale. This is when the cost of producing each additional unit goes down as you produce more. On the other hand, if a business expands too quickly without sufficient demand, the cost per unit may increase due to inefficiencies and wasted resources.  
  
Additionally, revenue per unit can also change as a business scales. For example, a company might be able to charge more per unit as its brand becomes better known and its products more desirable. Conversely, a company might need to lower its prices to attract a larger customer base, which could decrease revenue per unit.  
  
It's crucial to monitor and understand these changes in unit economics as a business scales, as they can significantly impact profitability. If the cost per unit rises faster than the revenue per unit, a company could find itself in a precarious financial situation. Conversely, if a company can increase its revenue per unit or decrease its cost per unit as it scales, it could significantly boost its profitability.

## CAC and LTV

CAC stands for Customer Acquisition Cost, which is the total cost of acquiring a new customer. This includes costs related to marketing and sales, such as advertising expenses, salaries for sales and marketing staff, and any other costs associated with gaining a new customer.  
  
LTV, on the other hand, stands for Lifetime Value. This is the total revenue that a company can reasonably expect from a single customer over the course of their relationship with the business. This includes all purchases that a customer makes, minus the costs of servicing them.  
  
The CAC-to-LTV ratio is a crucial metric for startups because it helps determine the overall profitability and sustainability of their business model. A healthy CAC-to-LTV ratio means that the cost of acquiring a new customer (CAC) is significantly lower than the total revenue that customer will generate over their lifetime (LTV). This indicates that the company's marketing and sales efforts are efficient and that the business model is sustainable in the long run.   
  
If the CAC is higher than the LTV, however, it means that the company is spending more to acquire a customer than they will earn from them. This is a red flag and could indicate that the business model is not sustainable. The company may need to adjust their marketing strategy, increase their prices, or find other ways to increase the LTV or reduce the CAC.

## What are investors looking for?

  1. A healthy LTV to CAC ratio. If you’re spending too much on acquiring a customer compared to how much they bring you, you’re in trouble.

  2. CAC-to-LTV ratio at scale. As your company scales, investors will want to see that you have a plan in place for improving the ratio: Spend less on acquiring a customer, make more money from them, or deliver the product more efficiently, i.e. lower your cost of goods sold.

  3. Realistic figures. You will need to be able to defend your numbers. 


## How do you tell this part of the story?

You don’t necessarily need a separate slide for this, but it’s important that you know the numbers. Many founders end up having a unit economics slide in the appendix. 

If you are building a product where the unit economics shift significantly at scale (most physical products— cars, dishwashers, gadgets—fall into this category), you’ll definitely want to include this. 

This part of your story is best illustrated using clear charts and graphs. The form of your graphics? Up to you! Just make sure that the figures are clear and that everything makes sense.

## What do you need to remember?

There is likely to be a lot of prediction involved in these figures. But that doesn’t give you licence to fly away with the fairies. They need to be believable and defendable. This is where you will demonstrate to potential investors how well you understand your business and how it fits into the existing business landscape. You’re going to have to be foot-perfect on your figures.

## Common pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Underestimating your marketing and advertising costs

It’s very easy to think that you can rely on viral marketing across social media and word-of-mouth to grow your customer base, but the reality is harsher than that. You are going to have to spend some money on raising brand awareness and getting your product into the hands of your customers. 

### Pitfall 2: You over-estimate your customer base

We get it; figuring out your churn rate and LTV is tricky. But you do need to be realistic about it. You need to be able to demonstrate that your customer numbers are growing at a rate that’s attractive to VCs, but at the same time, you can’t realistically predict that within a year the entire world will be your customer.

### Pitfall 3: You optimise for revenue too soon

In pursuit of a healthy CAC to LTV ratio, it can be tempting to optimise for revenue too soon. In short, you are going to need to spend more on customer acquisition than you might think is reasonable at first, but it’s what you need to get things rolling. Customers bring revenue. So spend on acquiring them at first, then allow things to even out.

## Summary

Unit economics might not be the most thrilling of topics, but they are so important in conveying the potential of your startup. Hone them, polish them, and then use them to dazzle your VCs.

## Who is your target customer?

When you define your problem, state your solution, and devise your product, you’re always working with a customer in mind. What’s the point of going to all that trouble of solving a problem for people who don’t perceive it as a problem and won’t therefore pay money to solve it? There’s no point in building your USB-powered Light Sabre display case and charger without a market of Light Sabre-armed Jedi to buy it. Your solution has to be addressing a real pain point for real people. 

## Why do you need to understand your target market?

Marketing costs money and for it to be effective, you need to be able to put the right message in the right place so that the intended people see it. By understanding your target customer you can:

### Define your value proposition

Your value proposition sets out the benefits that your product or service brings to your customer, and reinforces why they are prepared to spend money on it. 

The value proposition of a dinner box delivery subscription like Hello Fresh! isn’t the meals it puts on the table. That’s a feature. Rather, it’s the time it saves you thinking about what to cook and buying the ingredients and the money it saves by limiting wasted and unused ingredients.

### Inform your go-to-market strategy 

When you have a clear understanding of who your target customer is and the pain point your product is relieving, it allows you to devise an effective go-to-market strategy. It helps you direct your marketing efforts to the right places and send out the right message to your customers. You have to spend money to make money, but you don’t want to be spending money in the wrong places.

For example, if you manufacture big button mobile phones for seniors that accommodate limited mobility and sight impairment, you have a clear target customer. But reaching them might take several forms. You might use daytime TV to advertise to them directly, but you also might use the social media platforms where their children hang out. After all, they might want the peace of mind that comes with knowing their parents are always contactable with an easy-to-use mobile phone. 

## What are investors looking for?

  1. **That you know who your target customers are** \- You need to be able to identify a specific demographic for your investors to demonstrate that you understand your market clearly.

  2. **That you fully understand their pain points** \- Your customer needs to experience a very particular inconvenience that needs to be solved. What is it and why does it matter?

  3. **That your product meets their needs** \- Your product needs to be the painkiller that your target customer needs. It’s not good enough for it to be a nice-to-have vitamin.

  4. **That there’s a growing market of these customers** \- If you’re solving a problem specific to three mothers in Wisconsin, that’s absolutely brilliant for them, but your business isn’t venture scale. Your product needs to be solving a problem experienced by mothers of teenagers who attend after-school activities and fall into a particular income bracket.


## How do you tell this part of the story?

Think of your target customer. Give them a name and a photo. Identify how the problem they experience causes them pain, and how, in turn, your product relieves that pain. Now weave your customer’s story together with your overall narrative. Introduce them. Bring them to life. Go back to them throughout your pitch. Recall them. Make your investor audience believe in them, and with it the importance and viability of your product. And finally, ram home that while you’ve introduced your investors to one Janey down the road who’s desperate for your monthly stockings-through-the-mail supply, there are millions more Janeys out there. 

## What do you need to remember?

This part of the story is always benefits-driven. You are always looking to highlight the benefit that your product brings to your target customer. And that, by extension, will bring the benefits of profits and growth to your company and your VCs.

## Common pitfalls

You’ve probably seen these pitfalls rear their heads in other sections, too. Be alert: they are common and persistent.

  1. **Everyone’s a customer** \- No, they really aren’t. You need to identify a specific demographic you can target with your solution to their problem. 

  2. **Your customer base is too niche** \- If enough people aren’t experiencing the problem you’ve identified, then your market can’t grow. Without the opportunity for growth in your market, and significant, sustained growth at that, your business won’t interest VCs.

  3. **You’re not relieving a real pain for your customers** \- People need to experience the problem you’ve identified with sufficient acuity to be prepared to spend money on your solution.


## Summary

Benefits, benefits, benefits. What particular benefit does your business bring to a significant but distinctly identifiable group of people that will bring you growth, and eventually, a return on your VC’s investment?

It’s a little like being a journalist. It would help if you thought about the who, the what, the when, the where, the why, and the how.

## Exit strategy slide

Having an exit strategy slide can feel as tempting as the forbidden fruit hanging from the proverbial startup tree. It's a bit like the guy who talks about retirement plans on a first date—sure, long-term thinking is good, but there’s a time and a place, my friend, and now isn’t it. 

## **Why it shouldn’t be there**

The exit strategy is the "happily ever after" many founders dream of, whether they openly admit it or not. But there's a fine line between preparing for success and prematurely playing Queen’s "We Are the Champions" when you're still at the starting line. Unless you're a late-stage startup eyeing an IPO, having an exit slide is like a toddler sporting a full beard—it's just not the right stage for it.

### A waste of your precious time

Think about it. Investors know a thing or ten about exits. They've seen companies go public, get acquired, or implode like a bad soufflé. Do you really want to use your precious pitch minutes to lecture them on a topic they could teach at Harvard Business School? Exactly.

### It makes you look short-sighted

Your primary job is to make your startup irresistible, like a pie cooling on the windowsill in a cartoon. Saying upfront how you plan to sell that pie (your company, stay with me here) could signal that you're not fully invested in making it as scrumptious as possible.

### A crystal ball you don’t own

Let's face it, predicting your exit is like predicting the weather ten years from now. I've seen startups correctly guess who might acquire them but bungle the 'why' so badly it’s like shooting the basketball into the wrong hoop.

### VC math - It’s complicated

To VCs, your exit isn't just a casual Friday; it's the climax of a very expensive blockbuster film they've invested in. Selling your company for $10 million might make you dance, but to a VC aiming to return an entire fund, it's like finding pocket change down the back of a couch.

## **What if investors ask about it?**

That’s what we’d call a curveball question. If someone asks you about your exit strategy, your best bet is to wax poetic about your plans for an IPO while also being open to "reasonable acquisition offers" discussed with your board. This shows you’re not just looking to flip the company like a pancake, but that you're open to various breakfast options.

## **In short, you just don’t know**

Lastly, remember that at the early stages, you don't have a crystal ball (and if you do, let’s talk later). An exit strategy at this point is little more than a wild guess dipped in hubris sauce.

So, omit that exit strategy slide. Instead, focus on the compelling stuff that makes your startup the future unicorn everyone wants a ride on. And who knows, maybe one day you'll have the enviable task of actually planning that exit, sailing off into the sunset while your early investors build shrines in your honor.

## Appendices

The appendix is your stalwart sidekick to your main pitch deck. Not the bright luminary that steals the limelight, but the diligent librarian who pulls out the perfect tome at just the right moment. 

The appendix is there to expand on points you make in the main deck, if your audience wants to know more. It’s your indispensable contingency plan.

### **What are appendices for?**

Imagine this. Your main pitch deck is a streamlined spaceship, designed to get you from Earth to Alpha Centauri (that is, from 'Hi' to 'Investment') in under 30 minutes. Now, the appendix is like the ship's cargo hold, packed with extra fuel, space food, photon torpedoes, and a karaoke machine—just in case. 

In layman's terms, it's where you store the dense information that you don't want clogging up the sleek design of your main presentation. If a curious investor asks for more details on your quasar manipulation technology or your two-year marketing strategy, specific details about what type of AI is buzzing under your startup’s hood, or a hiring plan for your next 18-24 months, you're prepared to delve deeper without a hitch.

### **What content should be included?**

The appendix is a magical bag of holding that can accommodate a variety of additional material:

  1. **Complex graphics** : For those investors who like to see your entire business model represented as a sprawling subway map.

  2. **Text-heavy slides** : The bibliophiles, the scholars, the would-be lawyers—they'll want the fine print, and here is where you keep it.

  3. **In-depth product roadmaps** : Because some folks like to know every pit stop from Earth to the edge of the Milky Way.

  4. **Technical slides** : The microscopic details of your product that would appeal to a mad scientist or an engineer but would send a humanities major into hibernation.

  5. **Intellectual property strategies** : How you plan to legally defend your invention of the 'Sonic Unflappable Space Whisk.'

  6. **Detailed marketing strategies** : All the strategic nuances of capturing hearts, minds, and disposable income, broken down by channel.

  7. **Business model strategy** : This is like your ship’s manual, explaining how you intend to make money during your interstellar journey without resorting to space piracy.

  8. **Go-to-market plans by vertical or region** : Precisely where and when you’ll be launching your space whisk in the Andromeda galaxy and beyond.

  9. **Investment criteria charts** : A sort of matchmaking profile for investors, detailing the qualities you're looking for and the sort of commitment you're ready for.

  10. **Risk management strategies** : This is your protocol for asteroid fields, black holes, and rogue AI systems on board.

  11. **Competitor SWOT analysis** : A guide to the Galactic Empire of Competitors, and how you are training your Jedi skills to take them on.


### **Do I need an appendix?**

Do you need a spare tire when you embark on a cross-country road trip? Well, technically no, but you’d be a sitting duck without it when faced with a puncture in the middle of nowhere. The appendix is your safeguard, your contingency plan. If an investor wants to understand the economic viability of asteroid mining in your five-year plan, you can pull out the corresponding slide faster than you can say "Warp Speed!"

Not only does the appendix show you're prepared, it also subtly implies you're efficient. Your main deck remains a paragon of brevity, while the appendix says, "Hey, there's more to see, but only if you're really interested." It's like having a secret compartment in your spaceship for special guests, replete with vintage space wine and other cosmic delicacies.

### **In conclusion**

Look, this is space travel—and by space travel, I mean securing VC investment. Things get unpredictable. Sometimes you encounter nebulas of complexity, asteroid fields of tricky questions, or black holes of doubt that weren't on your star map. That's when the appendix becomes your ace up the sleeve, your towel on an intergalactic hitchhike, your friendly neighborhood wizard when you're up against a fire-breathing dragon. 

So why on Earth—or any other planet—would you ever consider leaving home without it?

Like a seasoned explorer with a utility belt or an adventurer with a well-packed bag, your appendix prepares you for the questions you didn’t even know were coming. It’s not just extra slides; it’s extra armor. In the world of startups, like in most good space operas, you never really know what's coming next. But with a well-prepared appendix, you can at least be sure you'll handle it with aplomb.

## You have to choose one: B2B or B2C

I was talking with a founder who shared that around 70% of their startup's sales were to consumers, and the rest was to businesses. An intriguing challenge in their dynamic startup journey, indeed. But how should they address this elusive story of their sales mix during their pitch, you may wonder?

Well, dear friends, the answer is quite different from what you might expect: **You don't**. Yes, you heard it here first! Whether your startup team comprises individuals with expertise in B2B or B2C, it's not about categorizing who's _purchasing_ your product. It's about _how you're marketing and selling_ that valuable product of yours.

## B2C Sales

B2C, or Business-to-Consumer sales, is a dynamic and essential aspect of the commercial landscape. In the B2C model, companies directly engage with individual consumers, offering a wide range of products and services that cater to their personal needs and preferences. This approach is characterized by its focus on creating appealing brand identities, persuasive marketing campaigns, and seamless customer experiences. B2C sales typically involve shorter sales cycles compared to B2B, as decisions are often made at an individual level without the complexities of corporate procurement processes.

One of the defining features of B2C sales is its emphasis on understanding and connecting with the emotions, desires, and aspirations of individual consumers. Successful B2C businesses invest heavily in market research and consumer behavior analysis to craft products and marketing strategies that resonate with their target audience. In the world of B2C, the customer is king, and building trust and loyalty through exceptional customer service and satisfaction is paramount.

Even if you are running a B2C playbook, you may occasionally make sales to companies. Those aren’t B2B sales this context; yes, the buyer was a company, but they bought after they went through your B2C sales efforts. Resist the temptation to treat them as B2B customers; they knew what they were buying, and as a startup you don’t have the resources to offer them B2B level customer support. 

## **B2B Sales**

B2B, or Business-to-Business sales, is a crucial component of the global economy, driving transactions between companies that provide products or services to one another. Unlike B2C sales, which involve individual consumers, B2B sales are often characterized by complex negotiations, long sales cycles, and high-value transactions. In this realm, the focus shifts from emotional appeal to practicality and cost-effectiveness. B2B sales involve catering to the specific needs and goals of businesses, ranging from small enterprises to large corporations.

In B2B sales, establishing strong, long-term relationships is fundamental. Building trust and credibility is paramount as companies rely on one another for ongoing success. Sales representatives often engage in detailed discussions and negotiations to ensure that the products or services being offered align with the purchasing company's objectives and provide tangible value. Customization and flexibility in solutions are highly valued in the B2B world, as companies seek partners that can adapt to their changing requirements.

It’s less common to make sales to consumers when you’re running a B2B playbook, but it could happen. Slack is fundamentally a B2B product, but it has self-service, and a lot of friend groups use Slack for group chats, party-planning, sharing holiday snaps, and, well, anything else.

## Which sales organization are you going to build?

In the context of a startup pitch, it isn’t really about who is going to be buying your products, but **how you’re going to run your sales and marketing**. This plays into your go-to-market strategy, and that’s the piece of the puzzle you need to get across to your potential investment. 

Pick one—and map that story out, both in terms of your go-to-market, sales, and cost of sales story.

# Part 6: The Fundraising Process

## How do I know when I'm ready to pitch to investors?

Knowing when you're ready to pitch to VC investors is a crucial milestone in your startup journey. And just like one does not simply walk into Mordor, one does not simply waltz into a VC office unprepared. So, how do you know when you’re ready? Let’s venture (pun intended) into this labyrinthine question.

First, let’s crack open the champagne and let the confetti rain down: your pitch deck will never be perfect! You heard me. It's like trying to catch a unicorn. The aim is not for perfection but for a state of 'good-enoughness,' that sweet spot where the essence of your business is presented in an understandable, engaging, and persuasive manner.

### **The council of elders: friends, family, and trusted confidants**

Before you go on this epic quest, it's a good idea to gather your council of elders—trusted friends, family, and advisors who can give you constructive feedback. Think of them as your own personal Justice League or Avengers, except instead of saving the world, they're helping you save face. 

Take heed, however! If your grandmother thinks you can do no wrong, her feedback will likely not be all that helpful even if it is heartwarming. On the other hand, if you have a sibling or friend who has a PhD in Brutal Honesty, their feedback might sting a bit but it’ll be invaluable.

### **The oracle speaks: Listen to VC feedback**

Now, if you've passed the preliminary round and started sending out your pitch deck, listen closely to the feedback you get from VCs. They're the experts in this arena. If they say they're only interested in Series A rounds, treat it as a learning experience about target audience selection. 

If, however, they express concerns about something within your control—say, the strength of your customer base—then you have the makings of an actionable roadmap. Don't discard this treasure trove of information; it's like finding a cheat code in a video game.

### **The rejection alchemy: Turn 'no' into nuggets of wisdom**

Rejections are as inevitable as death, taxes, and WiFi dropping during a crucial Zoom call. The trick is not to see them as terminal failures, but as signposts for future action. 

If a VC says something like, "You don't have enough traction," don't wallow in self-pity or pen your memoirs just yet. Instead, put that energy into building a better customer acquisition strategy. Each rejection is a breadcrumb on the path to venture capital enlightenment.

### **Your VC checklist: A list of must-haves before you pitch**

  1. **Validated business model** : Do you have paying customers? Are people interested in your product or service?

  2. **Competitive analysis** : Do you understand your market, including who your competitors are and how you’re different?

  3. **Financials** : Not just an Excel sheet, but a solid understanding of your numbers. Can you say "runway" without thinking of fashion?

  4. **Growth strategy** : Can you articulate how you plan to scale the business? (Hint: "Going viral on TikTok" is not a reliable strategy.)

  5. **Team** : Do you have the right mix of skills and experience within your team to execute your vision?

  6. **Legalities** : Is your intellectual property protected? Do you have any pending lawsuits that could pop up like a Whac-A-Mole?


### **The final sign: You can answer the tough questions**

You know you're ready when you can comfortably address tough questions from VCs without stuttering, sweating, or wishing for a time machine.

In short, knowing when you're ready to pitch is less about achieving a mythical state of perfection and more about doing your homework, listening to feedback, and preparing for the myriad questions that VCs will hurl your way. 

As they say, fortune favors the prepared, not the perfect. So gather your advisors, heed the VC oracles, and polish that pitch deck until it shines like the top of the Chrysler Building. Then, and only then, will you be ready to enter the exhilarating, nerve-wracking, and potentially life-altering realm of VC pitching. Onward!

## Who should be pitching to the VCs?

In the high-stakes theater of venture capital fundraising, the spotlight, whether you like it or not, is squarely on the founders—ideally, the CEO. It's not the time for understudies, side characters, or your friendly neighborhood salesperson to take center stage. 

Think of this as the great solo in a Broadway show; the lead singer doesn't just hand off the mic to a background dancer, no matter how charming their jazz hands are.

### **The triumvirate of CEO duties**

In the infancy of a startup, the CEO is the jack-of-all-trades—the chef, the waiter, and even the dishwasher. But as a company ripens like a fine cheese or an over-ambitious sourdough starter, the CEO’s role gets distilled into three primary duties. These are to:

  1. **Cultivate culture** : Infusing the company with its values and vision.

  2. **Manage human resources** : Recruiting the right talent to convert coffee into code—or whatever your company does.

  3. **Oversee the finances** : Ensuring the company doesn't go bankrupt. Like a dragon sitting on a pile of gold, but with less fire-breathing.


It's this third point that's crucial when it comes to fundraising. The CEO is the most intimately connected with the company's financial health, when seen in concert with everything else that’s going on within the organization: Product, marketing, sales, operations, everything. 

Handing off this role to anyone else raises questions faster than a caffeine-addicted auctioneer. What does it imply if the CEO isn’t equipped to raise funds?

### **Investors**

Investors are not merely ATMs with trust issues. They're looking for ongoing relationships with their investments. They want the inside scoop, the regular updates, and the option to whip out their Rolodex when you need a high-value introduction. It’s sort of like dating, but with more spreadsheets and less romance. For board members, their involvement often transcends to a whole other level of strategic input and oversight.

### **The equity equation and cap table calamities**

Another crux is the equity stake. Founders usually hold significant chunks of it, making them deeply invested in the company’s success or failure. It's akin to owning a slice of the pie—nobody else is going to care as much about its taste, texture, and marketability. When a non-founder approaches investors, it brings up existential questions:

  1. **Stake-less wandering** : If the pitcher doesn’t own a substantial piece of the company, why should the investor build a long-term relationship with them?

  2. **Cap table anarchy** : If a salesperson does hold a significant equity stake, it screams "cap table mismanagement!" from the rooftops. A well-managed cap table is like a well-tuned orchestra, and throwing a tuba solo into a string quartet is just not done.


### **Red flags**

To put it as clearly as a skywriter on a cloudless day: a pitch coming from someone other than the founders, and in the best-case scenario the CEO, flaps red flags so vigorously you could almost feel the wind from them. Investors, a breed already tuned to sniff out risks like bloodhounds on a scent trail, will not take kindly to such glaring deviations from the norm.

In summary, if you're the CEO and not pitching to VCs yourself, it's a bit like a conductor missing their own concert to watch it from the audience. Not only are you missing out on the action, but the orchestra might just decide to play an entirely different tune. So, pick up that metaphorical baton and lead your startup in the grand symphony of venture capitalism.

## How to find the right investors

Knowing how to find the right investors and get your business idea in front of them can feel more like an ordeal involving juggling flaming chainsaws while simultaneously tap-dancing than, well, compiling a list. But that’s just what you’re doing. Albeit a highly refined list. 

### **Identifying your investor strike zone**

Let's start by clarifying what you're actually looking for in an investor, shall we? This is what we'll call your "strike zone."

**Location, location, location:** Investors are like plants; they tend to thrive in certain geographical locations. Some are indigenous to the Silicon Valley ecosystem, while others flourish in the German financial terroir. Tailor your search according to your startup's geographic profile. If your business says it's European but the CEO and the legal paperwork are snuggled up in San Francisco, you’re as European as a California roll.

**Niche and affinity:** Investors can be terribly specific. Some may favor Stanford alumni, some might invest exclusively in hardware products aimed at the oil and gas industry. Others again might lean towards startups founded by wizards. Well, not wizards, but you get the point. Find out if you have some sort of "special sauce" that would make a particular group of investors swoon.

**Industry and business model:** This is about the skeleton of your business. Are you a B2C company selling artisanal nose hair trimmers, or perhaps a B2SaaS (Yes, that’s Business to Software as a Service)? Know where you fit in, or you'll find yourself pitching your nose-hair grooming solution to investors interested in drone technology for sustainable farming.

**Which stage are you at?** Is it pre-seed, seed, or Series A? To translate: Are you an idea on a napkin, a prototype in a garage, or a working model looking to scale? Know the difference. Each stage attracts a different breed of investor, from the nurturing angel to the hard-nosed venture capitalist.

**The money game:** Here, your fundraising round can be as ambiguous as a cubist painting, but you still need to define how much money you're raising clearly. Tie this to a business plan, or at least a list of "cool stuff we'll do with money" and match it with the investors' typical check sizes.

### **Unearthing potential investors**

**Step 1: Make a list of similar companies** **-** Why? Because if Investor X invested in a company that's a lot like yours, they may be more inclined to entertain the notion of investing in you. Just don’t pick your arch-nemesis in the market, as investors seldom back direct competitors.

**Step 2: Utilize fancy platforms** **-** Get on PitchBook, Crunchbase, TechCrunch, or the Wall Street Journal if you're old-school, to find who invested in the companies on your list. Make sure you note down the size of the round and the partner who led the deal. They’re your new best friends. Well, hopefully.

**Steps 3 & 4: Similar companies and expand** **-** Dive into the "Similar Companies" section in your tool of choice to find more investors and companies. A sign of a match made in heaven? Seeing the same names pop up like an overeager jack-in-the-box.

**Step 5: Prioritize** **-** Who's active? Who's a key player? Think of this like dating. You're not just swiping right on anyone; you're looking for 'The One.' Or, you know, maybe a few 'Ones' - we’re not all that into monogamy when it comes to investors.

**Step 6: Use available hubs** **-** Crunchbase and other platforms offer hubs where you can find companies similar to yours. Yes, these databases can sometimes miss the mark, like a weather forecast that promises sunshine but delivers a monsoon. However, it’s another tool in your toolbox.

**Step 7: Qualifying and outreach -** By the end of this, you'll have a list of investors longer than your weekly grocery list. Use that data to qualify them based on your initial criteria, then you can either warm up your network for intros or go in cold. Either way, the right preparation will make it much less likely that you’ll be left in the cold.

So yes, it's a slog, but with the right research and a pinch of panache, you can make your investment quest a narrative worth telling around future corporate campfires.

## How to get in front of the right investors

While it might feel practically impossible to get in front of VC investors, it’s not. I promise. Rather, it requires a meticulous blend of social alchemy, digital sleuthing, and a sprinkle of unabashed audacity.

### **Warm introductions**

If you’re fortunate enough to know an investor or a founder who’s successfully raised capital, congratulations! You’re already ahead of the game—sort of like starting a board game with a "skip to GO and collect $200" card. Ask these well-connected folks if you can pick their brains over coffee, a Zoom call, or perhaps a casual game of lawn chess (investors love lawn chess). Use this opportunity to humbly ask for introductions to potential investors. And remember, be specific about what you're looking for so they can tailor their recommendations.

### **LinkedIn**

  1. **Create a hit list** : Just as the Ghost of Christmas Future showed Scrooge a list of life choices to avoid, so too should you make a list—of your preferred investors, that is.

  2. **Second-degree sleuthing** : Drill down on LinkedIn to identify second-degree connections who can introduce you to your chosen investors. Think of it as LinkedIn's version of "Six Degrees of Kevin Bacon," but with fewer movies and more business plans.

  3. **Elevator pitch via email** : Write a brief yet impactful email to your second-degree connections. Explain your startup and why their help in making an introduction could be the cosmic alignment your venture needs.


### **Cold emailing**

You might quiver at the thought, but cold emailing is an art, and much like interpretative dance, it's underappreciated but can be highly effective.

  1. **Be a research ninja** : Don your digital cloak and dig deep into the portfolios of investors. Discover if they have a penchant for businesses in your sector and at your stage.

  2. **Social media stalking** : Go beyond LinkedIn to Twitter, personal blogs, and interviews. Gather investor-specific wisdom, sort of like collecting Easter eggs, but less chocolaty and more financially viable.

  3. **Crafting the email** : Think of it as a four-paragraph magic spell.

     * **Paragraph one** : Establish that you've done your homework on their investment patterns. Don’t be creepy, but be detailed.

     * **Paragraph two** : Unveil the wonder that is your startup, emphasizing why this specific investor should care. Toss in something they've said in the past to show you're not sending a form email. It’s flattery without the flowers.

     * **Paragraph three** : The ask. How much are you raising? Why? Align it with the investor's thesis like aligning planetary bodies for a cosmic event.

     * **Paragraph four** : Attach a one-pager summarizing why your startup is the next unicorn, minus the mythical powers. End with a call to action—just don’t phrase it like a late-night infomercial.


### **In conclusion: It’s the art of being brief but unforgettable**

Whether you're networking your way to a warm introduction or plunging into the icy waters of cold emailing, remember that brevity is your friend. Investors are busy people, often juggling numerous portfolios, board meetings, and at least one amateur winemaking hobby. Your goal is to get on their radar without becoming a blip they want to ignore.

By being meticulous in your approach and tailoring your communication, you increase the odds of not just catching their eye, but also captivating their interest. Soon, you might find yourself not just in front of a VC investor, but sitting beside them on the roller coaster ride of scaling your startup. Hold on tight; it's going to be exhilarating!

# Part 7: Hiccups and How to Overcome Them

## Traction - what if I don’t have revenue yet?

You’re an early stage startup. Revenue is on the horizon but not yet attainable. Still, you know that you’re onto something and you want to convince some investors that they can be onto your hot thing, too. 

So what can you use to demonstrate that things are definitely going in the right direction, even if the cash registers aren’t ringing yet? Well, traction is a multifaceted gem, shimmering in more colors than simply the green of dollar bills. There are plenty of alternatives to cold, hard cash.

* * *

Need a refresher on terms and acronyms before we go any further? Take a peek at the [glossary](<https://founders.pitch.guide/c/glossary/the-pitch-guide-fundraising-glossary>).

* * *

## 1\. Embrace the art of de-risking 

You started with an assumption as fragile as a house of cards balanced on a jittery squirrel. Let's face it, every startup begins life more as a hypothesis than a proven fact. 

What investors relish, aside from espressos and exit strategies, is the notion of de-risking. In other words, which risky business assumptions have you managed to whittle down to benign little toothpicks? Have you moved from "Wouldn't it be cool if..." to "Look, it's actually cool because..."? Chart that journey and you've got traction.

## 2\. Prototypes and MVPs: The papier mâché of business 

A minimum viable product (MVP) is the startup equivalent of a stand-in actor—an early version of your product that doesn't have to win awards, but has to be convincing enough. Take, for example, the case of building an air ticketing service. 

You might not have the capital to go full-Elon-Musk and revolutionize the whole industry overnight, but can you manually facilitate bookings and make your users' lives a smidgen easier? If yes, then investors won't mind if your MVP is held together by duct tape, wishes, and a sprinkle of audacity. And if it fails, then it’s told you something useful about your business.

Examples of MVPs that you can use include:

  * Mock landing pages on a website

  * Surveys

  * Videos


## 3\. Test the waters, don't wait for the tsunami 

If you've built an MVP, or what we can grandly call a "concierge version" of your future empire, then gather data. Every interaction with a customer is like dipping your toes into the water, each providing a temperature reading. Are they warm or cold to your offering? Each micro-transaction, user engagement, or even a finished test phase is a small but significant triumph. Sure, you might not be drowning in revenue, but you're also not drowning, period.

## 4\. Do you have patents? 

If you’ve succeeded in securing patents or some kind of intellectual property (IP) protection for functions that are critical to the success of your startup, then that’s traction. It’s part of the de-risking story, because it shows that someone else can’t come and eat your lunch.

## 5\. The questions investors ask: Your personal oracle 

Investors are basically human lie detectors mixed with clairvoyants. They ask questions like "How long until profitability?" not just to torment you, but because they're genuinely worried about the holes in your business model. These questions are gold. They reveal what keeps these money-guardians up at night (besides caffeine). Address these questions directly in your traction slide, even if your answers aren't draped in dollar signs. Show that you're not just waiting for problems to solve themselves—otherwise known as the "Please, Universe, Make It Go Away" strategy, which has a known success rate of approximately never.

## 6\. Traction isn't just about you; It's about them, too 

Traction is also a game of perception. Remember, investors are not just investing in a business, they're investing in you. Your ability to understand, navigate, and mitigate risks speaks volumes about your skills as an entrepreneur. So even if your traction slide doesn’t include soaring profits, it can still showcase a founder who is in tune with their business, empathetic to concerns, and proactive about solutions.

In short, while a flood of revenue would be terrific, a well-constructed raft can also impress, particularly if the alternative is sinking. Show that you've done your homework, have learned something valuable, and have maneuvered your startup a few crucial inches from the abyss. Because that is traction, plain and simple.

## It’s all about de-risking

Traction, for startups, signifies the increasing potential for success and a diminishing business risk. It is evidence that the startup's strategies are effectively working. In the early stages of a company, founders begin with certain resources, including mental capital, social connections, and initial financial capital. But they also face immense risk as they operate based on a series of assumptions about their business model, market demands, technical feasibility, and other factors. 

To show traction, startups need to validate their highest-risk assumptions. While robust revenue is a clear indicator of many assumptions being proven right, there are multiple other ways to demonstrate traction without immediate revenue generation. For instance, technical hurdles can be addressed by creating prototypes, and regulatory concerns can be pre-emptively managed by collaborating with experts. One practical method to prove traction without complete product development is to build a 'concierge MVP'. By manually performing certain backend tasks while testing the product's demand, startups can validate their business assumptions.

Engaging with potential investors is crucial to understand their concerns about the business. By actively seeking feedback and recognizing which aspects of the business investors are most apprehensive about, founders can then design experiments to address those concerns and present these findings as traction. While demonstrating rapid user and revenue growth remains the gold standard, showing traction in these alternative ways is valuable. It not only indicates the business's potential but also reflects the founder's comprehension of the risks and their proactive approach to mitigating them.

## Wait, was that a 'no'?

As founders on the fundraising trail, you’ll soon learn to spot a ‘soft no’. Basically, there’s rarely any incentive for an investor to write you off altogether. In fact, the only time that really happens, is if you are ‘out of thesis’, i.e. if your company is creating MedTech gadgets but this investor only invests in Crypto. Or the investor focuses on only Stanford graduates, and you are one of those MIT kids. 

VCs interact with countless entrepreneurs and hear myriad pitches. Yet, despite the number of interactions, only a few startups get funded. While some rejections might be direct, many times, entrepreneurs hear a 'soft no' rather than a direct decline.

## Why ‘Come back when you have more revenue?’

I often talk to pitch coaching clients who are annoyed and confused when they get the ‘come back when you have more revenue’ or ‘see us when your ARR hits $1 million’ and similar responses. During their research, they have spotted this investor putting money into companies which haven’t fulfilled those criteria, so what gives? 

Basically, the investor likes your company, but has decided there’s some reason not to invest. Maybe they have doubts about the team, about the company’s ability to sell, or something else, but they don’t want to say that out loud. However, that those reasons can be overcome with traction. If your team is mediocre but you are doing $10 million of revenue? The team is not mediocre. If your product is crappy but you’re attracting customers like there’s no tomorrow? Your product isn’t crappy. Traction forgives all sins, and it’s easier to say ‘come back when you have more traction’—which is hard to argue with—than to say ‘your team doesn’t have what it takes’. 

## Deciphering a soft ‘no’

Sometimes, the reason for the ‘no’ can be pretty obvious. Pay very close attention to the questions you’re being asked: If they drill in on your unit economics, and later turn you down, that may be a hint… 

At other times, the no is woven into a lace handkerchief making it less obvious to unpick. So, here are some of the reasons you may get a soft ‘no’: 

**Preserving relationships** : The startup ecosystem is surprisingly small. Today's rejected entrepreneur might be tomorrow's unicorn founder. By offering a soft no, VCs are trying to let the entrepreneur down easy, ensuring that bridges aren’t burned and relationships remain intact for potential future collaborations.

**Avoiding direct criticism** : Direct feedback can sometimes come off as harsh or demotivating, especially when the entrepreneur is deeply passionate about their idea. A soft no allows VCs to convey their lack of interest without potentially discouraging the founder from pursuing their vision.

**Market uncertainty** : The world of startups is fluid and dynamic. A startup that might not seem attractive to a VC today could pivot or evolve its business model in a few months, turning into a lucrative investment opportunity. By not shutting the door entirely with a hard no, VCs leave room for potential future discussions should circumstances change.

**Limited information** : Often, VCs base their decisions on the information available at a given time. A soft no allows them the flexibility to change their decision if more information becomes available later that might alter their initial assessment.

**Avoiding potential controversies** : Direct rejections, especially when based on gut feelings or intangibles, can lead to misunderstandings. A founder might interpret a rejection as bias or feel that the VC didn’t understand their pitch. A soft no can help mitigate these risks.

**Time constraints** : VCs are inundated with pitches. They may not always have the time or resources to conduct a deep dive into every startup that approaches them. In such cases, instead of giving a definitive answer, they might opt for a soft no, allowing them to revisit the opportunity if circumstances permit.

**Competitive landscape** : The VC world is highly competitive. By giving a soft no, VCs ensure they don’t send a strong signal to competing firms that a startup is unworthy of investment. If a VC flatly rejects a startup, it might discourage other potential investors from even considering the opportunity.

**Softening the blow** : VCs understand that entrepreneurs pour their heart and soul into their startups. A direct no can be crushing. VCs, many of whom have been entrepreneurs themselves, use soft nos as a way to show empathy and respect for the founder’s efforts.

**Open-ended opportunities** : Sometimes, while a VC might not be interested in investing, they see other potential avenues of collaboration—be it through networking, introductions, or mentorship. A soft no, as opposed to a direct rejection, keeps these avenues open.

**Potential conflicts of interest** : Occasionally, a VC might be in discussions with, or has already invested in, a startup that is in direct competition with the pitching startup. To avoid revealing too much or creating tension, a VC might resort to a soft no.

The 'soft no' can be frustrating for entrepreneurs who prefer clear feedback and direction, but it's a practice deeply rooted in the nuances and intricacies of the venture capital world. 

Entrepreneurs should interpret such responses as an opportunity to refine their pitches, seek feedback elsewhere, and perhaps re-approach VCs at a later stage or after significant milestones have been achieved. On the other hand, VCs should strive to be as transparent as possible without compromising on their existing commitments or discouraging both founders and other investors. This ensures that the startup ecosystem remains healthy, supportive, and constructive.
